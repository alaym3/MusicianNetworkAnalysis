'''Ian Richard Moss''' (born 20 March 1955) is an Australian rock musician from [[Alice Springs]]. He is the founding mainstay guitarist and occasional singer of [[Cold Chisel]]. In that group's initial eleven year phase from 1973 to 1984, Moss was recorded on all five studio albums, three of which reached number&nbsp;one on the national [[Kent Music Report]] Albums Chart. In August 1989 he released his debut solo album, ''[[Matchbook (Ian Moss album)|Matchbook]]'', which peaked at number&nbsp;one on the [[ARIA Charts|ARIA Albums Chart]]. It was preceded by his debut single, "[[Tucker's Daughter]]", which reached number&nbsp;two on the related ARIA Singles Chart in March. The track was co-written by Moss with [[Don Walker (musician)|Don Walker]], also from Cold Chisel. Moss had another top&nbsp;ten hit with "[[Telephone Booth (song)|Telephone Booth]]" in June 1989.

At the [[ARIA Music Awards of 1990]] Moss won five categories: [[ARIA Award for Album of the Year|Album of the Year]], [[ARIA Award for Best Male Artist|Best Male Artist]], [[ARIA Award for Breakthrough Artist – Album|Breakthrough Artist – Album]], [[ARIA Award for Single of the Year|Single of the Year]] and Breakthrough Artist – Single. Since then his solo music career has been more low-key, his other top&nbsp;50 albums are ''[[Worlds Away (Ian Moss album)|Worlds Away]]'' (November 1991), ''[[Let's all Get Together]]'' (July 2007) and ''[[Soul on West 53rd]]'' (November 2009). In [[ARIA Music Awards of 1993|1993]] Cold Chisel, with Moss as a member, were inducted into the [[ARIA Hall of Fame]]. Moss has participated with periodic Cold Chisel reunion tours or new studio albums in 1998, 2003 and from 2009 to 2012 (as from August 2013).

==Career==
===Early life===
Ian Richard Moss was born on 20 March 1955 in [[Alice Springs]], [[Northern Territory]], to Geoffery Moss (18 April 1911 – 1989) and Lorna ([[Name at birth|née]] Robinson) (born ca. 1922).<sup id="nbFoot01a" class="reference">[[#noteFoot01a|[A]]]</sup> During World War II Geoffery survived the [[Bombing of Darwin|1942 bombing of Darwin]], he enlisted in the army and later worked for the [[Allied Works Council]] in Alice Springs.<ref name="PlaceNames">{{cite web | url = http://www.ntlis.nt.gov.au/placenames/view.jsp?id=23855 | title = Place Names Register Extract: Geoff Moss Bridge | publisher = [[Government of Northern Territory]] | access-date = 6 August 2013 }}</ref><ref name="WW2 Roll">{{cite web | url = http://www.ww2roll.gov.au/VeteranCertificate.aspx?VeteranID=844724 | title = Certificate for Geoffery Moss | publisher = [[Department of Veterans' Affairs (Australia)|Department of Veterans' Affairs]]. [[Government of Australia]] | access-date = 6 August 2013 }}</ref> Lorna was a fellow employee at Allied Works Council when they married in September 1945.<ref name="PlaceNames"/><ref name="NLA1"/>

Moss has an older brother, Peter Geoffrey Moss (born 8 October 1948),<ref name="MossChisel"/><ref name="NLA2">{{cite news | url = http://nla.gov.au/nla.news-article43787053 | title = Family Notices | newspaper = [[The Advertiser (Adelaide)|The Advertiser]]|location=Adelaide | date = 14 October 1948 | access-date = 5 August 2013 | page = 8 | via = [[National Library of Australia]] }}</ref> an older sister, Penny (born 30 July 1951), and a younger brother, Andrew (born Jul 13th 1961).<ref name="MossChisel"/><ref name="NLA3">{{cite news |url=http://nla.gov.au/nla.news-article59838248 |title=Family Notices | newspaper = Centralian Advocate |location = [[Alice Springs]], NT | date = 3 August 1951 | access-date = 5 August 2013 | page = 4 | publisher = [[National Library of Australia]] }}</ref><ref name="Catterns">{{cite web | url = http://www.abc.net.au/reslib/200807/r268571_1125924.mp3 | title = Gillian Calvert and Ian Moss | last = Catterns | first = Angela | author-link = Angela Catterns | work = Conversation Hour | publisher = [[ABC Local Radio|ABC Local]] ([[Australian Broadcasting Corporation]]) | date = 7 July 2008 | access-date = 10 August 2013 }} Note: Ian Moss interview starts at about half-an-hour.</ref> When Moss was four&nbsp;years-old he performed for his family and friends and at nine, sang "[[The Battle of New Orleans]]" at a school concert.<ref name="MossBio"/><ref name="Hamey"/> He later recalled "I was always keen on music and singing. I used to do little vocal concerts for my parents".<ref name="Hamey"/> Moss initially took piano lessons but switched to guitar at age 11:<ref name="Kennedy">{{cite web | archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20080119114252/http://www.abc.net.au/southwestwa/stories/s1242563.htm | url = http://www.abc.net.au/southwestwa/stories/s1242563.htm | title = Ian Moss from Cold Chisel to Solo Career | last = Kennedy | first = Sharon | publisher = [[ABC South West WA]] ([[Australian Broadcasting Corporation]] (ABC)) | archive-date = 19 January 2008 | date = 12 November 2004 | access-date = 9 August 2013 }}</ref> "My older sister was learning classical piano and my older brother was a good rhythm strummer and right into Bob Dylan. I started off with classical piano aged about 7 or 8, but unfortunately wasn't into it enough".<ref name="MossBio"/>

In 1969 he joined a local band, The Scene, which included drummer Rod Martin and two brothers, Robert and John Fortunaso.<ref name="Holmgren">{{Cite web | archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20130220092517/http://hem2.passagen.se/honga/database/m/mossian.html | url-status = dead | url = http://hem2.passagen.se/honga/database/m/mossian.html | publisher = [[Australian Rock Database]]. Passagen (Magnus Holmgren) | title = Ian Moss | last1 = Holmgren | first1 = Magnus | archive-date = 20 February 2013 | access-date = 13 February 2014 | year = 2007 }}</ref> Moss played rhythm guitar by plugging an acoustic guitar into the bass player's amplifier. The Scene played at local centres and organised their own dances. Moss sang a couple of songs with the group and a year later, he bought an electric guitar to replace his acoustic. Moss recalled, "I was with them for about a year and by that time I was starting to get my own band happening".<ref name="MossChisel">{{cite web | archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20041214192155/http://www.coldchisel.com.au/barkspid/1/tbs2_6_8.htm | url = http://www.coldchisel.com.au/barkspid/1/tbs2_6_8.htm | title = All Alone Playing Rock Solo Ian Moss | publisher = Official Cold Chisel Website | archive-date = 14 December 2004 | access-date = 5 August 2013 }}</ref>

The following year he formed Hot Ice with Roger Harris – guitar, David Michel – drums, Wayne Sanderson – Bass and Paul Wiles – keyboards.<ref name="Holmgren"/> The group debuted in front of the whole school at the Alice Springs High School regular Friday assembly which took place in the Alice Springs Youth Centre with cover versions of [[Creedence Clearwater Revival]]'s "[[Proud Mary]]" and "[[Who'll Stop the Rain (song)|Who'll Stop the Rain]]".<ref name="MossChisel"/> There after they regularly played at the Youth Centre Saturday night dances. 
<ref name="MossChisel"/> In 1972, Moss relocated to Adelaide – where his older siblings had already moved. Moss had failed a year of high school and decided to repeat at [[Marion, South Australia#Community facilities|Marion High School]]. In 1973 he started an Electronics course at Kilkenny Technical College but "lasted one term there&nbsp;... totally daydreaming the whole time", he left and worked in various factory jobs.<ref name="Catterns"/>

===1973–1984: Cold Chisel===
{{Main|Cold Chisel}}
In September 1973 Ian Moss, on guitar and lead vocals, formed a rock group, Orange, in Adelaide with [[Don Walker (musician)|Don Walker]], a songwriter and [[electronic organ|organist]], and Leszek Kaczmarek on bass guitar.<ref name="HolmgrenCC">{{Cite web | archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20070712040930/http://hem2.passagen.se/honga/database/c/coldchisel.html | url-status = dead | url = http://hem2.passagen.se/honga/database/c/coldchisel.html | publisher = [[Australian Rock Database]]. Passagen (Magnus Holmgren) | title = Cold Chisel | last1 = Holmgren | first1 = Magnus | last2 = Shoppee | first2 = Philip | archive-date = 12 July 2007 | access-date = 13 February 2014 | year = 2007 }}</ref><ref name="McFarlaneCC">McFarlane, {{webarchive |url=https://web.archive.org/web/20040419101605/http://www.whammo.com.au/encyclopedia.asp?articleid=213 |date=19 April 2004 |title='Cold Chisel' entry }}. Archived from [http://www.whammo.com.au/encyclopedia.asp?articleid=213 the original]  on 19 April 2004. Retrieved 5 August 2013.</ref><ref name="Nimmervoll">{{cite web|url=http://www.howlspace.com.au/en/coldchisel/coldchisel.htm |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20010221222413/http://www.howlspace.com.au/en/coldchisel/coldchisel.htm |url-status=dead |archive-date=21 February 2001 |title=Cold Chisel |work=Howlspace |last=Nimmervoll |first=Ed |author-link=Ed Nimmervoll |publisher=White Room Electronic Publishing Pty Ltd |access-date=5 August 2013 |df=dmy-all }}</ref> Within months the line-up included [[Steve Prestwich]] on drums and [[Jimmy Barnes]] on lead vocals, and they were renamed, [[Cold Chisel]].<ref name="Hamey"/><ref name="HolmgrenCC"/><ref name="McFarlaneCC"/><ref name="Nimmervoll"/> The group's early line-up was volatile with Barnes, Prestwich and Walker all leaving and returning at various times.<ref name="McFarlaneCC"/><ref name="Nimmervoll"/> In 1975 Kaczmarek was replaced on bass guitar by [[Phil Small]].<ref name="HolmgrenCC"/><ref name="McFarlaneCC"/><ref name="Nimmervoll"/>

Moss's main role was as lead guitarist, although he would frequently take over lead vocals when Barnes was out of the band.<ref name="McFarlaneCC"/><ref name="Nimmervoll"/><ref name="Creswell">{{cite book| last1 = Creswell | first1 = Toby | author-link1 = Toby Creswell | title = Too Much Ain't Enough | publisher = [[Random House]] | location = Sydney, NSW | pages = 87–99 | year = 1993 | isbn = 978-0-09-182818-9}}</ref> After periods spent in Adelaide, [[Armidale, New South Wales|Armidale]] and Melbourne, Cold Chisel eventually settled in Sydney in mid-1977 and signed a recording contract with [[Warner Music Group|WEA]] late that year.<ref name="McFarlaneCC"/><ref name="Nimmervoll"/> Early the following year they recorded their [[Cold Chisel (album)|self-titled debut album]], which appeared in April.<ref name="HolmgrenCC"/><ref name="McFarlaneCC"/><ref name="Nimmervoll"/> Moss supplied lead vocals for two tracks, "One Long Day" and "Rosaline".<ref name="Cold">{{cite AV media notes | title = [[Cold Chisel (album)|Cold Chisel]] | others = [[Cold Chisel]] | year = 1978 | publisher = [[Warner Music Group|WEA]] | id = 600038 }}</ref>

[[File:Ian Moss at the Cold Chisel last stand tour, 1983.jpg|thumb|right|300px|Moss on the Cold Chisel Last Stand Tour, 1983]]
Moss's vocals feature on some of the band's best known songs, including the singles "[[My Baby (Cold Chisel song)|My Baby]]" (August 1980), "[[When the War Is Over]]" (1982), and "[[Saturday Night (Cold Chisel song)|Saturday Night]]" (March 1984) and on ''[[Circus Animals]]'' (March 1982) album track, "Bow River".<ref name="McFarlane">McFarlane, {{webarchive |url=https://web.archive.org/web/20040628154910/http://www.whammo.com.au/encyclopedia.asp?articleid=516 |date=28 June 2004 |title='Ian Moss's entry}}. Archived from [http://www.whammo.com.au/encyclopedia.asp?articleid=516 the original] on 28 June 2004. Retrieved 5 August 2013.</ref> Moss wrote "Bow River"<ref name="APRA Bow">{{cite web | publisher = [[Australasian Performing Right Association]] (APRA) | title = 'Bow River' at APRA search engine | url = http://www.apra-amcos.com.au/worksearch.axd?q=Bow%20River | access-date = 5 August 2013 }}</ref> about a [[cattle station]] in the Kimberley region of Western Australia where his brother, Peter, had once worked; it has since become Moss's signature tune.<ref name="Lynch">{{cite web | url = http://www.triplem.com.au/sydney/music/news/2012/4/listen-cold-chisel-legend-ian-moss-explains-story-behind-bow-river-photo/ | title = Cold Chisel Legend Ian Moss Explains Story Behind 'Bow River' Photo | last = Lynch | first = Jo | publisher = [[Triple M Network]] | date = 5 April 2012 | access-date = 5 August 2013|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20141224044624/http://www.triplem.com.au/sydney/music/news/2012/4/listen-cold-chisel-legend-ian-moss-explains-story-behind-bow-river-photo/|archive-date=24 December 2014 }}</ref> It was also the B-side of "[[Forever Now (Cold Chisel song)|Forever Now]]", which was released in the same month as the album.

Moss sang lead on the group's version of "[[Georgia On My Mind]]" which became a staple of their live shows, although their only recording is on the 1984 live album, ''[[Barking Spiders Live: 1983]]''. Their fifth studio album, ''[[Twentieth Century (Cold Chisel album)|Twentieth Century]]'', was issued in April 1984 with two tracks, "Saturday Night" and "Janelle", featuring Moss on lead vocals.<ref name="McFarlaneCC"/> However the group had disbanded as Barnes was keen to begin his solo career.<ref name="McFarlaneCC"/><ref name="Nimmervoll"/>

During Cold Chisel's first phase, Moss gained status as a songwriter, contributing "Never Before"<ref name="APRA Never">{{cite web | publisher = [[Australasian Performing Right Association]] (APRA) | title = 'Never Before' at APRA search engine | url = http://www.apra-amcos.com.au/worksearch.axd?q=Never%20Before | access-date = 6 August 2013 }}</ref> for ''[[East (Cold Chisel album)|East]]'' (June 1980), the track was the first one aired by national radio station [[Triple J]] when it switched from AM to FM in 1980. Another Moss-written track is "No Good for You" on ''Circus Animals''.<ref name="APRA No Good">{{cite web | publisher = [[Australasian Performing Right Association]] (APRA) | title = 'No Good for You' at APRA search engine | url = http://www.apra-amcos.com.au/worksearch.axd?q=No%20Good%20for%20You | access-date = 6 August 2013 }}</ref> He also worked as a session musician for other artists,<ref name="Holmgren"/> including playing a guitar solo on the track "Skin" from the album ''[[Icehouse (album)|Icehouse]]'' (October 1980) by Sydney [[New wave music|new wave]] band [[Icehouse (band)|Flowers]] and an appearance on [[Richard Clapton]]'s ''The Great Escape'' (March 1982),<ref name="Holmgren"/> which also featured Barnes.

===1985–1996: Solo career success===
After Cold Chisel disbanded, Ian Moss took a break from the music industry before launching his solo career in October 1986.<ref name="McFarlane"/> His first backing band consisted of Andy Cowan on keyboards (ex-[[Madder Lake (band)|Madder Lake]], [[Ayers Rock (band)|Ayers Rock]], [[Kevin Borich|Kevin Borich Express]]), Joe Imbroll on bass guitar (ex-[[Goanna (band)|Goanna]]) and John Watson on drums (ex-Kevin Borich Express, [[Australian Crawl]]).<ref name="McFarlane"/> By April the following year the line-up was Watson, Joe Creighton on bass guitar (ex-Melissa, Billy T) and Peter Walker on guitar and synthesiser (ex-[[Bakery (band)|Bakery]])<ref name="McFarlane"/> – Walker had also produced Cold Chisel's debut album in 1978.<ref name="HolmgrenPW">{{Cite web | archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20070713181356/http://hem2.passagen.se/honga/database/w/walkerpeter.html | url-status = dead | url = http://hem2.passagen.se/honga/database/w/walkerpeter.html | publisher = [[Australian Rock Database]]. Passagen (Magnus Holmgren) | title = Peter Walker | last1 = Holmgren | first1 = Magnus | archive-date = 13 July 2007 | access-date = 13 February 2014 | year = 2007 }}</ref>

During 1988 Moss worked in Los Angeles for nine months preparing material for his debut album, ''[[Matchbook (Ian Moss album)|Matchbook]]''.<ref name="McFarlane"/> In November 1988, Moss released his debut single, "[[Tucker's Daughter]]" which peaked at number&nbsp;two on the [[ARIA Charts|ARIA Singles Chart]] and number&nbsp;six on the [[Recorded Music NZ|New Zealand]] Singles Chart.<ref name="NZCharts">{{cite web | url = https://charts.nz/showinterpret.asp?interpret=Ian+Moss | title = Discography Ian Moss | publisher = New Zealand Charts Portal. Hung Medien (Steffen Hung) | last = Hung | first = Steffen | access-date = 6 August 2013 }}</ref>

Moss's second single, "[[Telephone Booth (song)|Telephone Booth]]", was issued in June-1989 and reached number&nbsp;seven on the ARIA Singles Chart,<ref name="AusCharts">Australian (ARIA) chart peaks:
*Top 50 peaks: {{cite web | url = http://australian-charts.com/showinterpret.asp?interpret=Ian+Moss | title = Discography Ian Moss | publisher = Australian Charts Portal. Hung Medien (Steffen Hung) | last = Hung | first = Steffen | access-date = 6 August 2013 }}</ref> and top&nbsp;30 in New Zealand.<ref name="NZCharts"/> ''[[Matchbook (Ian Moss album)|Matchbook]]'', followed in August, which reached number&nbsp;one on the ARIA Albums Chart, it remained at the peak spot for three&nbsp;weeks<ref name="McFarlane"/><ref name="AusCharts"/> and sold more than 200,000 copies.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.coldchisel.com/band/ian-moss/|title= Ian Moss|publisher=Cold Chisel|access-date=17 January 2018}}</ref> In New Zealand it reached the top&nbsp;20 on the albums chart.<ref name="NZCharts"/> ''Matchbook'' was produced by Moss and Lord-Alge; it was dedicated "in loving memory of Geoff Moss 1911–1989".<ref name="Matchbook">{{cite AV media notes | title = [[Matchbook (Ian Moss album)|Matchbook]] | others = Ian Moss | year = 1989 | publisher = [[Mushroom Records]] | id = TVL93307 }}</ref> Australian musicologist, [[Ian McFarlane]], found the album displayed Moss's "exemplary, controlled guitar technique, but also highlighted his soulful voice&nbsp;... [he] handled all lead vocals with considerable style and authority".<ref name="McFarlane"/>

Two further singles, "[[Out of the Fire (song)|Out of the Fire]]" and "Mr. Rain" were released from the album and both charted on the Australian top&nbsp;100.<ref name="McFarlane"/><ref name="AusCharts"/><ref name="AusCharts1989">{{cite web | url = http://australian-charts.com/forum.asp?todo=viewthread&search=Ian+Moss&id=34608&pages=#1095693 | title = Top Singles of 1989 | publisher = Australian Charts Portal. Hung Medien (Steffen Hung) | access-date = 6 August 2013 }}</ref> At the [[ARIA Music Awards of 1990]] Moss won five categories [[ARIA Award for Album of the Year|Album of the Year]], [[ARIA Award for Best Male Artist|Best Male Artist]] and [[ARIA Award for Breakthrough Artist – Album|Breakthrough Artist – Album]] for ''Matchbook''; and [[ARIA Award for Single of the Year|Single of the Year]] and Breakthrough Artist – Single for "Tucker's Daughter".<ref name="McFarlane"/><ref name="ARIA1990">{{cite web | url = http://www.ariaawards.com.au/history/year/1990 | title = ARIA Awards – History: Winners by Year 1990: 4th Annual ARIA Awards | publisher = [[Australian Recording Industry Association]] (ARIA) | access-date = 6 August 2013 }}</ref><ref name="TallP1990">{{cite web | url = http://www.alldownunder.com/australian-music-aria/1990-aria-awards.htm | title = 1990 ARIA Award Winners | publisher = ALLdownunder.com | access-date = 6 August 2013 }}</ref> His touring band in support of ''Matchbook'' consisted of Ian Belton on bass guitar (ex-[[QED (band)|QED]]), Guy Le Claire on rhythm guitar(Mar-Sept 1989)(ex-[[Eurogliders]]), Randy Bulpin on rhythm guitar (ex-[[Mondo Rock]]), Danny D'Costa on keyboards, Steve Fearnley on drums and Mark Williams and Mary Azzopardi on backing vocals.<ref name="McFarlane"/> 
[[File:Ian Moss.jpg|thumb|Moss at the [[ARIA Hall of Fame]] in Melbourne in July 2008.<br />At the [[ARIA Music Awards of 1990]] Moss won five categories [[ARIA Award for Album of the Year|Album of the Year]], [[ARIA Award for Best Male Artist|Best Male Artist]] and [[ARIA Award for Breakthrough Artist – Album|Breakthrough Artist – Album]] for ''Matchbook''; and [[ARIA Award for Single of the Year|Single of the Year]] and Breakthrough Artist – Single for "Tucker's Daughter".<ref name="McFarlane"/><ref name="ARIA1990"/><ref name="TallP1990"/> In [[ARIA Music Awards of 1993|1993]] Cold Chisel, with Moss as a member, were inducted into the Hall of Fame.<ref name="HoF2008">{{cite web | url = http://www.ariahalloffame.com.au/inductees_listing.htm | title = ARIA 2008 Hall of Fame Inductees Listing | publisher = [[Australian Recording Industry Association]] (ARIA) | access-date = 7 August 2013 |archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20080615011737/http://www.ariahalloffame.com.au/inductees_listing.htm | archive-date = 15 June 2008 }}</ref><ref name="HoF2012">{{cite web | url = http://www.ariaawards.com.au/history/award/hall-fame | title = Winners by Award: Hall of Fame | publisher = [[Australian Recording Industry Association]] (ARIA) | access-date = 7 August 2013 }}</ref>]]

In October 1991 Moss released his second album, ''[[Worlds Away (Ian Moss album)|Worlds Away]]'', which featured "songs largely co-written with Don Walker in the soul/R&B vein".<ref name="McFarlane"/> It reached the top&nbsp;50 on both the ARIA and New Zealand charts.<ref name="AusCharts"/><ref name="NZCharts"/> The album provided three singles, "Slip Away" (May), "She's a Star" (September) and "Never Give Up" (November);<ref name="McFarlane"/> both "Slip Away" and "She's a Star" reached the ARIA Top&nbsp;100.<ref name="AusCharts1991">{{cite web | url = http://australian-charts.com/forum.asp?todo=viewthread&search=Ian+Moss&id=32916&pages=#1095613 | title = Top Singles of 1991 | publisher = Australian Charts Portal. Hung Medien (Steffen Hung) | access-date = 7 August 2013 }}</ref>

Also that year Moss played in Don Walker's band Catfish, contributing guitar to their album, ''Ruby''. Subsequently, he made guest appearances on albums by [[The Black Sorrows]]' ''Better Times'' (1992) on a track called "Ain't Love the Strangest Thing", Richard Clapton's ''Distant Thunder'' (1993), on Barnes' solo album, ''[[Heat (Jimmy Barnes album)|Heat]]'' (1993),<ref name="Holmgren"/> the first time he had worked with Barnes in ten years. In June 1994 he provided lead guitar on the title track of Don Walker's solo album, ''We're All Gunna Die'' (1995).<ref name="Holmgren"/><ref name="We're All">{{cite AV media notes | title = We're All Gunna Die | others = Don Walker | year = 1995 | publisher = Salt Records | id = Salt 01 }}</ref> In [[ARIA Music Awards of 1993|1993]] Cold Chisel, with Moss as a member, were inducted into the [[ARIA Hall of Fame]].<ref name="HoF2008"/><ref name="HoF2012"/>

Moss's third solo album, ''[[Petrolhead]]'', was released in August 1996, which was produced by Don Walker for TWA Records.<ref name="Holmgren"/><ref name="McFarlane"/> For this album Moss used Walker on keyboards with Paul DeMarco on drums (ex-[[Rose Tattoo]]), the late [[Ian Rilen]] on bass guitar (from Rose Tattoo & [[X (Australian band)|X]]), and Trent Williamson on harmonica.<ref name="McFarlane"/> McFarlane declared it was "his best-ever album&nbsp;... [by] a down'n'dirty blues-rock outfit&nbsp;... with gritty, hard-edged tracks&nbsp;... [and] finely honed guitar work".<ref name="McFarlane"/> Two singles were issued, "All Alone on a Rock" and "Poor Boy", but neither charted.<ref name="McFarlane"/> It was re-released the following year as ''Ian Moss Box Set'' with an additional live CD, ''Ian Moss Live'', the live disc was issued separately in 1998.<ref name="Holmgren"/><ref name="McFarlane"/> Those live tracks had been recorded during 1996 to 1997 on his national tour supporting ''Petrolhead'', with Rilen replaced by Paul Wheeler on bass guitar mid-tour.<ref name="Holmgren"/><ref name="McFarlane"/><ref name="NLA Live">{{Citation | author1 = Moss, Ian | author2 = TWA Records | title = Ian Moss Live | date = 1998 | publisher = TWA Records. [[National Library of Australia]] | url = http://trove.nla.gov.au/work/7573107 | access-date = 8 August 2013 }}</ref>

===1997–2004: Cold Chisel reunion===
From October 1997 to June 1998 Cold Chisel reunited to record a new studio album, ''[[The Last Wave of Summer]]'' (October 1998), and subsequently toured.<ref name="McFarlaneCC"/> In May 2003, another Cold Chisel tour, ''[[Ringside (Cold Chisel album)|Ringside]]'', resulted in a 2× CD live album of the same name in November.

===2005–present: Solo career continued===
In 2005 Moss released his fourth studio album, ''[[Six Strings]]'' on [[Liberation Music]].<ref name="Holmgren"/><ref name="NLA Six">{{Citation | author1 = Moss, Ian | title = Six Strings | date = 2005 | publisher = Liberation Blue. [[National Library of Australia]] | url = http://trove.nla.gov.au/work/21014730 | access-date = 7 August 2013 }}</ref> it was an acoustic album and included material from his time with Cold Chisel, his solo releases and cover versions of [[Jimi Hendrix]] and [[Creedence Clearwater Revival]] tracks.<ref name="Cox">{{cite news | url = http://www.abc.net.au/tasmania/stories/s1515024.htm | title = ''Six Strings'' by Ian Moss: Disc of the Week :: Music Review | last = Cox | first = Tim | publisher = [[ABC Local Radio|ABC Tasmania]] ([[Australian Broadcasting Corporation]]) | date = 23 November 2005 | access-date = 7 August 2013 }}</ref> To support the release Moss undertook an extensive joint tour of Australia co-headlining with [[Jon Stevens]] (ex-[[Noiseworks]], [[INXS]]).

Moss travelled to New York in August 2008 to record his next album, ''[[Soul on West 53rd]]'',<ref name="NLA Soul">{{Citation | author1 = Moss, Ian | title = Soul on West 53rd | date = 2009 | publisher = Mosstrooper, Liberation (distributor). He performed at the 2021 NRL Grand Final, singing "Flame Trees" alongside Kate Miller-Heidke, before performing his solo hit "Tucker's Daughter".

Ian Moss was in a ''de facto'' relationship with Australian actress [[Megan Williams (actress)|Megan Williams]]<ref name="Curley">{{cite web | url = http://www.hotsource.com.au/news/383/ | title = John Prior from Matt Finish | last = Curley | first = Wendy Lloyd | publisher = Hotsource | date = 7 December 2006 | access-date = 9 August 2013 | quote = There's Megan Williams, the actress from ''The Sullivans'' who was with Ian Moss.
