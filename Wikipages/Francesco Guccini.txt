'''Francesco Guccini''' ({{IPA-it|franˈtʃesko ɡutˈtʃiːni|lang|Francesco guccini pronunciation.ogg}}, born 14 June 1940) is an Italian singer-songwriter, considered one of the most important ''[[Singer-Songwriter#Cantautori.2C the Italian tradition|cantautori]]''. During the five decades of his music career he has recorded 16 studio albums and collections, and 6 live albums. He is also a writer, having published autobiographic and [[Hardboiled#Noir|noir]] novels, and a [[comics writer]]. Guccini also worked as actor, soundtrack composer, [[lexicography|lexicographer]] and [[dialectology|dialectologist]].

Guccini moved to [[Pàvana]] during World War II, then returned to [[Modena]] where he spent his teenage years and established his musical career. His debut album, ''[[Folk beat n. 1]]'', was released in 1967, but the first success was in 1972 with the album ''[[Radici (album)|Radici]]''. He was harshly criticised after releasing ''[[Stanze di vita quotidiana]]'', and answered his critics with the song "L'avvelenata". His studio albums production slowed down in the nineties and 2000s, but his live performances continued being successful.

His lyrics have been praised for their poetic and literary value and have been used in schools as an example of modern poetry. Guccini has gained the appreciation of critics and fans, who regard him as an iconic figure.<ref name="le"/> He has received several awards for his works; [[39748 Guccini|an asteroid]], a cactus species and a butterfly subspecies have been named after him. The main instrument in most of his songs is the acoustic guitar.

A leftist, though not a [[communist]], Guccini dealt with political issues and more generally with the political climate of his time in some songs, such as "La Locomotiva" or "Eskimo".

== Origins ==
Guccini was born in 1940 in [[Modena]], Italy.<ref name=le4/> His father from [[Tuscany]], Ferruccio Guccini, was a postal employee, and his mother from [[Emilia (region of Italy)|Emilia]], Ester Prandi, was a housewife. While his father served in the Italian military during [[World War II]], Guccini lived with his grandparents in a small village in the [[Apennine Mountains]] in northern [[Tuscany]] called [[Pàvana]], where he spent his childhood.<ref name=or1/> His years spent in the somewhat archaic society of the mountains of [[Central Italy]] was to be a strong inspiration throughout his career, and it became one of the key recurring themes of his songs and books.<ref name=or2/>

When World War II had ended, Guccini moved back to his family in Modena.<ref name=or4/> He studied at the Istituto Magistrale Carlo Sigonio, the same school [[Luciano Pavarotti]] had attended, earning his high school diploma in 1958.<ref name=or3/> Guccini spent his teenage years in [[Modena]], as he later recounted in his second novel ''Vacca d'un Cane'' and in songs including "Piccola Città", which paints a bitter portrait of the city as "a strange enemy".<ref name=or5/>

== Youth and musical beginnings ==
[[File:Francesco Guccini La Locomotiva.JPG|thumb|Francesco Guccini singing "La&nbsp;Locomotiva"]]
Guccini's first job was as a teacher at a boarding school in [[Pesaro]], but he was fired after a month and a half. He then worked as a journalist at the [[Gazzetta di Modena]] for two years.<ref name=ymfj/> In April 1960, Guccini interviewed [[Domenico Modugno]],<ref name=ymbd/> who had just won two consecutive [[Sanremo Music Festival|Sanremo Festivals]]. This inspired Guccini to write "L'antisociale", his first composition as a singer-songwriter.<ref name=ymb1/><ref name=ymb2/>
In 1958 Guccini was guitarist and vocalist in a group first called Hurricanes, then Snakers and finally Gatti.<ref name=ymbhs/> The group included Pier Farri (drums), who would later become Guccini's producer; Victor Sogliani (saxophone), future member of [[Equipe 84]]; and Franco Fini Storchi (guitar). Guccini wrote his first songs while in the Snakers, in a style inspired by [[The Everly Brothers]] and [[Peppino di Capri]]. The group performed for two years, touring around Northern Italy and Switzerland.<ref name=ymb3/> In 1961 the Guccini family moved to [[Bologna]], and Francesco enrolled at the [[University of Bologna]] to study foreign languages. The next year he undertook mandatory military service, an experience he described as "substantially positive".<ref name=ymms/> When he returned to Bologna, Guccini was asked to join the band [[Equipe 84]], but he declined to continue his studies.<ref name=ymcs/> He later quit university just before taking his degree (he was conferred a [[degree honoris causa]] in [[science education]] in 2002).<ref name=awa7/> The band Cantacronache was an important influence in Guccini's artistic growth,<ref name=ymic/> as was [[Bob Dylan]].<ref name=ymbb/>

== Debut (1967–1969) ==
Record Producer [[Compagnia Generale del Disco|CGD]] commissioned Guccini to write a song for the 1967 [[Sanremo Festival]], "Una storia d'amore", to be sung by [[Caterina Caselli]] and [[Gigliola Cinquetti]] at . The song, though, was not selected for the event,<ref name=de1/> and Guccini was embittered by the edits made by two lyricists engaged by CGD.<ref name=de2/>
Guccini made his debut as a singer-songwriter in March 1967, with the album [[Folk beat n. 1]],<ref name=fbn1/> which received little commercial success.<ref name=leo/> Three of the songs recorded for the album had previously been successes for [[Nomadi]] and Equipe 84: "Noi non-ci saremo", "L'antisociale" and "[[Auschwitz (song)|Auschwitz]]". The latter was translated and sung in English by Equipe 84, as well as by [[Rod MacDonald]] in his 1994 album ''Man on the Ledge''.<ref name=rmcd/> Another song from the album, "In morte di S.F.", later renamed "Canzone per un'amica", was recorded by Nomadi in 1968.  From 1965 onward, Guccini spent 20 years teaching Italian at the off-campus [[Dickinson College]], in Bologna.<ref name=leC/>

In May 1967 Guccini made his first appearance in television, on ''Diamoci del tu'', hosted by [[Caterina Caselli]] and [[Giorgio Gaber]] singing "Auschwitz".<ref name=dmdt/> He wrote several songs for Caselli and for [[Nomadi]], who made his song "Dio è morto" become widely popular; it became one of his most famous songs, despite being censored by the [[RAI]] for [[blasphemy]].<ref name=nmdm/> In 1968 Francesco Guccini translated [[Simon & Garfunkel]]'s hit song "Mrs. Robinson" into Italian; it was first covered in this version by the Italian [[Beat music|beat]] group {{ill|I Royals|it}} and later was recorded by [[Bobby Solo]] on his LP Bobby Folk in 1970. In 1968 the [[Gramophone record|45&nbsp;rpm record]] ''Un altro giorno è andato/Il bello'' was released; Guccini re-recorded the Side A song in an acoustic version for his 1970 album ''[[L'isola non-trovata]]''. His first concert was held the same year at the La Cittadella Cultural Centre in [[Assisi]].<ref name=1stc/>

==1970s==
[[File:Bologna-DSCF7190.JPG|thumb|left|Via Paolo Fabbri 43, the address in Bologna after which Guccini's album is named.]]
In 1970 Guccini released his second album, ''[[Due anni dopo]]'', recorded in the autumn of 1969. The main themes of the album are the passage of time and the analysis of everyday life in the context of bourgeois hypocrisy,<ref name=dap2/> with a noticeable influence from French music<ref name=dap1/> and from [[Giacomo Leopardi|Leopardi]]'s poetic style.<ref name=dap/> After this album Guccini started his 10-year-long collaboration with folksinger Deborah Kooperman, who played [[fingerstyle guitar]] on it, a style mostly unknown in Italy at the time.<ref name=dkfs/> Eleven months after ''Due anni dopo'', the album ''[[L'isola non-trovata]]'' was released. The title was a literary reference to [[Guido Gozzano]], and the song "La collina" contained a reference to [[J. D. Salinger]].<ref name=ggjs/> Guccini's fame began to spread beyond Bologna, partly thanks to the appearance in the TV show ''Speciale tre milioni'', where he sang some of his songs and befriended [[Claudio Baglioni]].<ref name=sptm/> In 1971 he married his long-time girlfriend Roberta Baccilieri, who was pictured on the back cover of his next album.<ref name=mar1/>

The turning point in Guccini's career was in 1972 thanks to the album ''[[Radici (album)|Radici]]'' (roots), about the perpetual search for one's origins. This was also conveyed by the image on the front cover of the album, portraying Guccini's grandparents and their siblings next to their old mountain home.<ref name=or2/> ''Radici'' contains some of his most renowned and popular songs,<ref name=radi/> like "Incontro", "Piccola Città", "Il vecchio e il bambino", "La Canzone della bambina portoghese", "Canzone dei dodici mesi", and "La locomotiva", based on a real event and dealing with themes of equality, social justice and freedom, with a style similar to the anarchic music of the end of the 19th century.<ref name=ra1/><ref name=pol3/> In the same year Guccini brought [[Claudio Lolli]], a young singer-songwriter, to his record label, [[EMI]] Italiana.<ref name=loll/> He later wrote two songs with him, "Keaton" and "Ballando con una sconosciuta".

In 1973 Guccini released ''[[Opera buffa]]'', a light-hearted and playful album, which showed his skills as an ironic, theatrical and cultured [[cabaret]] artist.<ref name=cab1/> Guccini was perplexed by the release of the disc, especially because of its arrangements and because it was recorded live (with overdubs made in a recording studio).<ref name=opbu/> One year later ''[[Stanze di vita quotidiana]]'' was released, with a mixed reception by fans and critics.<ref name=sdvq/> It included six long and melancholic songs, a mirror of the crisis Guccini faced, worsened by constant disagreements with his producer Pier Farri.<ref name=pf/> Guccini received harsh criticism, including a slating by the critic Riccardo Bertoncelli, who said the singer songwriter was "a finished artist, who has nothing else to say".<ref name=ribe2/><ref name=ribe3/> Guccini answered with the song "L'avvelenata", a few years later.<ref name=lavv/>

Guccini had his first commercial success in 1976, with ''[[Via Paolo Fabbri 43]]'', which was the sixth best-selling album of the year.<ref name="seB"/> It was named after the address of the house in [[Bologna]] where he lived. He sang with a more mature and determined voice, and the musical structure was more complex than in his earlier works.<ref name=voic/> The album contained "L'avvelenata", a bitter and colourful reply to the criticism he received for ''Stanze di vita quotidiana'', which cited one of his critics, Riccardo Bertoncelli.<ref name=ribe3/><ref name=lavv/> Later Guccini was reluctant in performing the song during concerts, saying it was obsolete.<ref name=obso/><ref name=obso2/> {{Listen

The title track was an abstract description of Guccini's life in [[Bologna]], which referenced [[Jorge Luis Borges|Borges]] and [[Roland Barthes|Barthes]]; it also mentioned the "three heroines of Italian song", Alice, Marinella and Lilly, three women from songs by Italian singer-songwriters [[Francesco De Gregori|De Gregori]], [[Fabrizio De André|De André]] and [[Antonello Venditti|Venditti]].<ref name=vpf/> Other notable tracks were "Canzone quasi d'Amore", characterised by existential poetry,<ref name=fdpg/> and "Il pensionato", about an old neighbour of Guccini, focusing on the sad psychological situation of some old people.<ref name=vpf/> Guccini's next album, ''[[Amerigo (album)|Amerigo]]'' was released in 1978. The most popular song was "Eskimo", but Guccini claimed the highest point was the title track, a ballad about an emigrant uncle of his.<ref name=vpf4/>
In 1977 the weekly magazine ''Grand Hotel'' featured Guccini on the cover titled "The father every teenager would have liked to have". Guccini did not endorse the article, which was based on an interview he did not know would be published,<ref name="se"/> and commented: "I cannot understand how they chose that title, I write songs for an audience of people in their thirties, I do not see how an audience of sixteen year olds fresh out of school could relate with the things I say".<ref name="seA"/> In the same year, Guccini separated from his wife Roberta (the song "Eskimo" is about this event),<ref name=se7/> and started cohabitating with Angela. In 1978 they had a daughter, Teresa, to whom the songs "Culodritto" and "E un giorno..." are dedicated.<ref name=coh1/> In 1979 the live album ''Album concerto'', recorded in a concert with [[Nomadi]], was released. It was peculiar because the songs were performed in duet with [[Augusto Daolio]], and because it included previously unreleased songs: "Dio è morto", "Noi", and "Per fare un uomo".<ref name=voic/>

== 1980s ==
[[File:Francesco Guccini 2.jpg|thumb|left|Francesco Guccini on stage]]
The first album released by Guccini in the eighties was ''[[Metropolis (Guccini album)|Metropolis]]'', which was characterised by the description of cities with a symbolic value: [[Byzantium]], Venice, [[Bologna]] and [[Milan]]. Their histories mingle with the distress caused by life in the city and with symbolic references.<ref name=metr/> The album had richer arrangements, with saxophones, bass guitars, drums, [[clarinets]], flutes and [[zuffolo|zufoli]].<ref name=ei1/> Byzantium is described by Guccini as a fascinating yet oppressive city at the crossroads of two continents and two eras. The song is set at the time of Emperor [[Justinian I]] (483–565), and there are many historical references to that period, that have been explained by Guccini himself.<ref name=metr2/> The narrator, Filemazio, who some critics believe to be a fictionalized Guccini,<ref name="ei"/> senses the decadence of his civilization and the coming of the end. The song was praised by critic Paolo Jachia, who described it as "moving and dreamlike".<ref name="ei"/> In 1981 Guccini was the co-author, along with [[Giorgio Gaber]], Sandro Luporini and Gian Piero Alloisio, of the musical ''Gli ultimi viaggi di Gulliver''.<ref name=guvg/> The eponymous song "Gulliver" was then included in Guccini's next album, ''[[Guccini (album)|Guccini]]'', which dealt with the same themes found in ''[[Metropolis (Guccini album)|Metropolis]]''. Notable songs in the album include "Shomèr ma mi llailah?" ("Watchman, what of the night?", from {{bibleverse|Isaiah|21:11}}),<ref name=bibl/> "Autogrill", about a love only dreamt of, and "Inutile", which narrates a day two lovers spent in [[Rimini]].<ref name=auto/> The subsequent tour was the first in which Guccini performed with a [[backing band]]; previously, Guccini used to perform solo, or with just one or two guitarists.<ref name=baba/> In 1984 the live album ''Fra la via Emilia e il West'' was released. It included live versions of many of his popular songs, recorded mainly at a concert held in [[Piazza Maggiore]] in [[Bologna]], in which several guests performed alongside Guccini: [[Giorgio Gaber]], [[Paolo Conte]], [[I Nomadi]], [[Roberto Vecchioni]] and [[Equipe 84]].<ref name=tvew/>

In 1987 the album ''[[Signora Bovary]]'' was released. Several of the songs portray people from Guccini's life: "Van Loon" is his father, "Culodritto" is his daughter Teresa, and "Signora Bovary" is himself.<ref name="tvew"/> Other songs include "Keaton", written with his friend [[Claudio Lolli]], and "Scirocco", an award-winning song about an episode in the life of the poet Adriano Spatola, a friend of Guccini.<ref name=qcn/> In 1988 the singer-songwriter released a live album, ''[[...quasi come Dumas...]]'', which included some of his songs from the Sixties, in a rearranged version. The title is a homage to ''[[Twenty Years After]]'', the novel by [[Alexandre Dumas]].<ref name=twya/>

== 1990s ==
In 1990 Guccini released ''[[Quello che non...]]'', which continued in the style of ''[[Signora Bovary]]''.<ref name=qcn/> Songs included in the album are "Quello che non" and "La canzone delle domande consuete", which received the [[Club Tenco]] best song of the year award.<ref name="tenco"/> Three years later, he released ''[[Parnassius Guccinii]]'' referencing a subspecies of butterfly which was named in his honour.<ref name=awa9/> The song "Farewell", included in the album, is a homage to [[Bob Dylan]]'s "[[Farewell, Angelina (song)|Farewell, Angelina]]", featuring its instrumental introduction and citing a verse ("The triangle tingles, and the trumpet plays slow").<ref name=90B/> The literary critic Paolo Jachia commented: "Guccini's enormous poetic and cultural effort has been opening the best tradition of Italian poetry to Dylan-esque ballads".<ref name=90A/> Other songs included in the album are "Canzone per Silvia", dedicated to Silvia Baraldini, and "Acque", composed for [[Tiziano Sclavi]]'s movie ''Nero''.

It was three years until he released his next album, ''[[D'amore di morte e di altre sciocchezze]]'', which achieved significant commercial success.<ref name=90C/> Tracks included are "Cirano", inspired by the play ''[[Cyrano de Bergerac (play)|Cyrano de Bergerac]]''; "Quattro stracci", about the ending of the relationship with Angela (the same woman to whom ''Farewell'' was dedicated);<ref name=90E/> "Stelle" about the feelings of powerlessness men feel when looking at the starry night sky; "Vorrei", dedicated to his new partner, Raffaella Zuccari, and "I Fichi", a farcical song.<ref name=90D/><ref name=90if/>

== 2000s ==
''[[Stagioni]]'' was Guccini's first album of the 2000s. The key theme is the passage of time and the different temporal cycles connected to it. Songs included are "Autunno", "Ho ancora la forza" (with [[Luciano Ligabue|Ligabue]]), "Don Chisciotte", in which Guccini takes the role of [[Don Quixote]], and his guitarist that of [[Sancho Panza]]), and "Addio", a song akin to "L'avvelenata".<ref name=stag/> The album and its tour were successful, with the unexpected presence of many young people among the audience, establishing Guccini as an iconic artist for three generations.<ref name=stai/> A special limited edition vinyl version of ''Stagioni'' was also released.
[[File:Guccini 2013.jpg|thumb|Francesco Guccini in 2013]]
In 2004 Guccini released ''[[Ritratti]]''. Some of the songs contained in the album are imagined dialogues with historical figures, such as [[Odysseus]], [[Christopher Columbus]] and [[Che Guevara]].<ref name=ritt/> The first track of the album, "Odysseus", is on the theme of travel, and contains references to the [[Odyssey]], to [[Dante Alighieri|Dante]] (Canto 26 of [[Inferno (Dante)|Inferno]]), and to a poem by [[Ugo Foscolo|Foscolo]].<ref name=odys/> Another song in the album, "Piazza Alimonda", is about the [[death of Carlo Giuliani]] during the [[Genoa G8 summit#Protests|demonstrations at the G8 summit in Genoa]].<ref name=pol1/> ''Ritratti'' received critical acclaim and commercial success, reaching the number one spot in the [[FIMI]] Albums Chart, holding it for two weeks, and remaining in the chart for eighteen weeks.<ref name=ritr/> In the same year, lyrics from "Canzone per Piero" were included in a final upper secondary school exam";<ref name=lep/><ref name=lex/> Guccini claimed he was "embarrassed and glad" about being alongside [[Cicero]] and [[Raphael]].<ref name=eds/>
In 2005 the live double-album ''Anfiteatro Live'', recorded in the [[amphitheatre]] in [[Cagliari]], was released; it also included a DVD of the concert. ''Anfiteatro Live'' was a commercial success, holding the number one spot in the FIMI Chart for one month, and remaining in the chart for twenty-two weeks.<ref name=anli/> In 2006 Guccini received one vote in the 2006 [[Italian presidential election, 2006|Italian presidential election]].<ref name=twpe/> The same year, the triple-album ''The Platinum Collection'', containing 47 songs, was released as a celebration of his fortieth year as a musical artist. In October Guccini's official biography, ''Portavo allora un Eskimo innocente'' by Massimo Cotto, was published.

On 21 April 2008, an article on [[La Stampa]] affirmed that Guccini had stopped smoking, and that this had caused him to gain weight and lose his inspiration.<ref name=twla/> He denied it on 18 May 2008, in TV show ''Che tempo che fa''. In 2010 the [[Mondadori]] published ''Non so che viso avesse'', a book which contains a Guccini autobiography and, in the second part of the book, a critical essay edited by Alberto Bertoni.<ref name=twns/> [[Luciano Ligabue]], friend and colleague of Guccini, entitled him a song, "Caro il mio Francesco"  on his album ''[[Ligabue discography|Arrivederci, mostro!]]''. On 28 September 2010 the collection ''Storia di altre storie''  was released, with songs selected by Guccini himself.<ref name=stst/> In the same year the [[botanist]] Davide Donati named a new species of Mexican cactus, the Corynopuntia guccinii, after him. In the article about the discovery on the botanical magazine ''Piante Grasse'', Donati explained that he discovered the unknown plant whilst listening to Guccini's "Incontro", adding: "I could not have named it after anyone else".<ref name=twco/> On 25 April 2011, Guccini married for the second time , with Raffaella Zuccari, who had been his partner in the last fifteen years.<ref name="twla-2"/>

==Style==
{{Quote box |align=right| width=30%|qalign=left|salign=right|quote  = Guccini is the voice of what was once called the "social movement". Now it's simply a voice of truth, of rock-like coherence with its own language and thoughts. In his works there's a never-ending discourse about irony, friendship and solidarity|source = [[Dario Fo]]<ref name=sty/> |quoted = 1 }}
Guccini's lyrical and poetic style has been praised by many, including famous authors and singer-songwriters.<ref name=sty/><ref name="leB"/><ref name="leA"/> Fellow singer-songwriter [[Roberto Vecchioni]] said about Guccini: "he's not a singer of stories, he's a singer of thoughts and a singer of doubts", while Nobel Prize winner [[Dario Fo]] called him a "voice of truth".

Despite the length of his career, there are some defining characteristics, such as the use of different [[Register (sociolinguistics)|registers]], the literary references to several writers, and the use of a variety of themes to reach moral conclusions. His lyrics frequently have a metaphysical tone and [[Existentialism|existential]] motifs, and are often centered around portrayals of people and events.<ref name=onda/> Guccini's voice is baritonal, with a noticeable [[Rhotacism (speech impediment)|rhotacism]]. Most of his songs, especially early in his career, are [[folk rock]].<ref name=leo/>

Guccini has been seen as a sociopolitical chronicler and some of his songs express his opinion about a political issue. In "La primavera di Praga", he expressed criticism of the [[Warsaw Pact invasion of Czechoslovakia|Sovietic occupation of Czechoslovakia]] in 1968 , and "Piccola storia ignobile" supported the [[Abortion in Italy|Italian abortion law]].<ref name=pdp/> "Canzone per Silvia" was dedicated to [[Silvia Baraldini]], and both "Canzone per il Che" and "Stagioni" were dedicated to [[Che Guevara]]. "Piazza Alimonda" was about the riots at the [[Genoa G8 summit|G8 summit]] in [[Genoa]] and "La locomotiva" was about the anarchic [[Pietro Rigosi]] running a locomotive at full steam towards death.<ref name=pol3/><ref name=pol1/>

Guccini defines himself an [[Anarchy|anarchic]],<ref name=pol/> and he expressed his thoughts about the relation between music and politics in his song "L'avvelenata"; "I have never said that with songs you can make revolutions, nor that you can make poetry."<ref name=avln/>("''però non ho mai detto che a canzoni si fan rivoluzioni, si possa far poesia.")''

== Books ==
[[File:Guccini1.jpg|thumb|Francesco Guccini in 2006]]
In his career as a writer, Guccini published several novels and essays, experimenting with different genres.<ref name=bkmv/> His first novel, ''[[Cròniche Epafàniche]]'', was published by [[Feltrinelli (publisher)|Feltrinelli]] in 1989,<ref name=boce/> and was one of his most successful works.<ref name=boce1/> Even though it is not explicitly an autobiography, it can be considered the first of three autobiographical books. It describes past events of [[Pàvana]], the town where he spent his childhood. Guccini recounts stories he heard from elderly people living on the [[Tuscany|Tuscan]] [[Apennines]]; critics praised the "philological accuracy" of the book.<ref name=boce2/><br />
His next two novels, ''[[Vacca d'un cane]]'' and ''[[Cittanòva blues]]'' were also bestsellers, and covered different periods of his life. ''Vacca d'un cane'' depicts a teenage Guccini in [[Modena]], as he realizes that the city's provincialism will be an obstacle to his intellectual growth,<ref name=bovc/> while ''Cittanòva Blues'' the last part of his trio of autobiographical books, tells of his time in [[Bologna]], seen as a "little Paris".<ref name=bocb/><ref name=bocb1/>
Guccini also collaborated with [[Loriano Macchiavelli]] for a series of [[Hardboiled#Noir fiction|Noir]] books, and published a Dictionary of the dialect of Pàvana which showed his ability as [[dialectologist]] and translator.<ref name=bkmv/><ref name=bo/>

Guccini has also worked as a [[comics writer]]. He is a lover of comics, and some of his songs reference them.<ref name=boco1/> He's been author and script writer of comics, such as ''Vita e morte del brigante Bobini detto "Gnicche"'', illustrated by Francesco Rubino, ''Lo sconosciuto'', illustrated by [[Roberto Raviola|Magnus]], and ''Cronache di spazio profondo'', drawn by his friend Bonvi.<ref name=boco3/><ref name=cmcs/>

== Cinema ==
Guccini's first experience as actor was in the 1976 movie ''Fantasia, ma non-troppo, per violino'', directed by Gianfranco Mingozzi, in which he played [[Giulio Cesare Croce]], a poet who narrates the history of [[Bologna]].<ref name=cigm/> He then appeared in: ''I giorni cantati'', a 1979 movie directed by [[Paolo Pietrangeli]], which featured two of Guccini's songs in its soundtrack, "Eskimo" and "Canzone di notte n°2"; ''Musica per vecchi animali'', a 1989 movie directed by Umberto Angelucci and [[Stefano Benni]];<ref name=ci1/> ''[[Radiofreccia]]'', the 1998 directorial debut of singer-songwriter [[Luciano Ligabue]];<ref name=cilg/> ''Ormai è fatta'', the 1999 movie directed by Enzo Monteleone.<ref name=cim/> In the 2000s he acted in three movies directed by [[Leonardo Pieraccioni]], ''Ti amo in tutte le lingue del mondo'' (2005),<ref name=cilp/> ''Una moglie bellissima'' (2007)<ref name=cil/> and ''Io & Marilyn'' (2009).<ref name=cip/> Guccini wrote the soundtrack of the 1977 movie ''[[Nenè]]'', directed by [[Salvatore Samperi]], and his song "Acque" featured in the soundtrack of ''[[Nero]]'', the 1992 movie directed by Giancarlo Soldi.<ref name=ci1/>

== Awards ==
[[File:Corynopuntia guccinii flower.jpg|thumb|The ''Corynopuntia guccinii'' flower]]
Awards, accolades and recognitions received by Guccini:
* In 1992 he received the Librex Montale Award "Poetry for Music", for the song "Canzone delle domande consuete".<ref name=awaA/>
* In 1992, new subspecies of [[butterfly]] on the Tuscan-Emilian [[Apennines]], was named ''[[Parnassius mnemosyne]] guccinii'' in his honor.<ref name=awa9/>
* In 1997 [[Luciano Tesi]] and [[Gabriele Cattani]] discovered an asteroid, naming it [[39748 Guccini]].<ref name=awa10/>
* In 2001 his story "La Cena" was included in the anthology "Racconti italiani del Novecento" (I Meridiani – [[Mondadori]]).<ref name=awa8/>
* In 2002 the universities of [[University of Bologna|Bologna]], [[University of Modena and Reggio Emilia|Modena and Reggio Emilia]] awarded him an honorary degree in [[Science of Education]]<ref name=awa7/>
