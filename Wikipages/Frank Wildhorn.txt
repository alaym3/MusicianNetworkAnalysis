'''Frank Wildhorn''' (born November 29, 1958) is an American composer of both  musicals and popular songs. His musical ''[[Jekyll & Hyde (musical)|Jekyll & Hyde]]'' ran for four years on Broadway. He also wrote the #1 International [[Hit single|hit song]] "[[Where Do Broken Hearts Go]]" for [[Whitney Houston]].

==Early years==
Wildhorn was born in Harlem and spent his childhood in [[Queens]] before moving to [[Hollywood, Florida]], at age 14.  Soon after he taught himself how to play the piano, Wildhorn realized he wanted to compose music.  During high school, he played in and wrote for various bands, ranging from rock and roll to [[Rhythm and blues]] to jazz. He attended [[Miami-Dade College]] for two years before transferring to the [[University of Southern California]], where he studied history and philosophy. He started writing ''[[Jekyll & Hyde (musical)|Jekyll & Hyde]]'' with [[Steve Cuden]], who was working at USC when Frank was a student.<ref name=musicianguide>[http://www.musicianguide.com/biographies/1608002845/Frank-Wildhorn.html Wildhorn biography], accessed February 4, 2009</ref> He is [[Jewish]].<ref>The Broadway Composer Critics Love To Hate – The Forward

==Career==
===Popular Music===
In the popular music arena, Wildhorn has worked with such artists as [[Stacy Lattisaw]], [[Natalie Cole]], [[Kenny Rogers]], [[Trisha Yearwood]], [[Tracy Lawrence]], [[Trace Adkins]], [[Patti LaBelle]], [[Dennis DeYoung]] and [[Linda Eder]], to whom he was married. His "[[Where Do Broken Hearts Go]]" was an international number one hit for [[Whitney Houston]] in 1988.
Wildhorn is Creative Director of Atlantic Theatre, an [[Atlantic Records]] division that develops new American musicals.<ref name=musicianguide/> In 2005, he co-founded [http://www.globalvisionrecords.com/ GlobalVision Records] with long-time collaborator Jeremy Roberts. GlobalVision releases include a new concept recording of ''[[Dracula, the Musical]]'' and a new studio recording of ''Jekyll & Hyde: Resurrection''.

===Stage===
In 1999, Wildhorn had three shows running simultaneously on [[Broadway theatre|Broadway]]: ''[[Jekyll & Hyde (musical)|Jekyll & Hyde]]'' at the [[Plymouth Theatre]], ''[[The Scarlet Pimpernel (musical)|The Scarlet Pimpernel]]'' at the [[Minskoff Theatre]], and ''[[The Civil War (musical)|The Civil War]]'' at the [[St. James Theatre]]; however, all three shows closed without making a profit, for a total loss approaching $20 million.<ref>{{cite news|last=McKinley|first=Jeffrey|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2000/11/10/movies/on-stage-and-off-jekyll-and-hyde-among-closings.html|title=ON STAGE AND OFF; 'Jekyll and Hyde' Among Closings|work=[[The New York Times]]|date=November 10, 2000}}</ref> In 2004, he collaborated with [[Don Black (lyricist)|Don Black]] and [[Christopher Hampton]] on a musical based on ''Dracula''.

Wildhorn has become a prominent composer globally, with many productions in Europe and Asia having long runs. Jekyll & Hyde is one of the longest running musicals of all time in South Korea. The musical ''[[Carmen (Wildhorn musical)|Carmen]]'', with music by Wildhorn and lyrics by Jack Murphy, premiered in Prague in October 2008.<ref>[http://www.frankwildhorn.com/projects/carmen/ "Carmen" information] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090106175625/http://www.frankwildhorn.com./projects/carmen/ |date=January 6, 2009 }} frankwildhorn.com</ref> The musical ''[[Count of Monte Cristo (musical)|Count of Monte Cristo]]'', music by Wildhorn with book and lyrics by Jack Murphy, received a workshop reading in November 2008, and opened at the Theatre of St. Gallen, Switzerland in March 2009.<ref>Gans, Andrew and Jones, Kenneth.[http://www.playbill.com/news/article/123086.html "Tale of Two Cities' Barbour and Burkhardt Cast in Monte Cristo Workshop"] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090209071147/http://www.playbill.com/news/article/123086.html |date=February 9, 2009 }}, playbill.com, November 6, 2008</ref><ref>Gans, Andrew.[http://www.playbill.com/news/article/126412-Borchert-to-Star-in-World-Premiere-of-Wildhorns-Count-of-Monte-Cristo Borchert to Star in World Premiere of Wildhorn's Count of Monte Cristo"] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121015075950/http://www.playbill.com/news/article/126412-Borchert-to-Star-in-World-Premiere-of-Wildhorns-Count-of-Monte-Cristo |date=October 15, 2012 }} playbill.com, February 18, 2009</ref> Another musical, with music by Wildhorn and lyrics by [[Don Black (lyricist)|Don Black]], ''Bonnie and Clyde'', received an industry reading in February 2009<ref>Jones, Kenneth.[http://www.playbill.com/news/article/125944.html Stark Sands and Laura Osnes are Bonnie and Clyde in NYC Reading of Wildhorn Musical"] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090207042051/http://playbill.com/news/article/125944.html |date=February 7, 2009 }}, playbill.com, February 4, 2009</ref> and premiered at the [[La Jolla Playhouse]] in California in November 2009. Also in November 2009, another new musical, ''[[Wonderland: Alice's New Musical Adventure]]'' premiered at the David A. Straz, Jr. Center for the Performing Art in [[Tampa]], Florida, with another production following at the [[Alley Theatre]], [[Houston]], Texas, in January 2010.<ref>Hetrick, Adam and Jones, Kenneth.[http://www.playbill.com/news/article/135978-Wildhorn-and-Murphys-Wonderland-Makes-Houston-Bow-Jan-15 "Wildhorn and Murphy's Wonderland Makes Houston Bow Jan. 15"] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100404073150/http://www.playbill.com/news/article/135978-Wildhorn-and-Murphys-Wonderland-Makes-Houston-Bow-Jan-15 |date=April 4, 2010 }} playbill.com, January 10, 2010</ref>

Wildhorn is also the first Broadway musician to work with the [[Takarazuka Revue]] on the Cosmos Troupe's production of the musical ''[[Never Say Goodbye (musical)|Never Say Goodbye]]''.

Other musicals include [[Camille Claudel (musical)|''Camille Claudel'']], which ran in 2003 at the [[Goodspeed Opera House]] and a short developmental run at the NYMT Festival in 2004, and ''Waiting for the Moon'' starring Lauren Kennedy and Jarrod Emmick in New Jersey in 2005, with the musical being renamed ''Zelda'' for a run at [[Flatrock Playhouse]] in 2012. In 2015, Wildhorn created a musical based on the popular manga series ''[[Death Note]]''. In 2018, he debuted ''The Man Who Laughs'' in South Korea, based on Victor Hugo's [[The Man Who Laughs|novel]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.broadwayworld.com/article/VIDEO-Frank-Wildhorn-Teases-THE-MAN-WHO-LAUGHS-in-Seoul-20180228|title=VIDEO: Frank Wildhorn Teases THE MAN WHO LAUGHS in Seoul|website=BroadwayWorld|date=February 28, 2018}}</ref> ''The Man Who Laughs'' won three awards at the 2019 Korean Musical Awards, including Best Musical.

==Personal life==
Wildhorn married [[Linda Eder]] on May 3, 1998. They have a son, and he has another son from an earlier marriage.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1998/05/03/style/weddings-linda-eder-frank-wildhorn.html|title=Weddings: Linda Eder, Frank Wildhorn|work=The New York Times|date=May 3, 1998}}</ref><ref>Jones, Kenneth. [http://www.playbill.com/news/article/87605.html "Frank Wildhorn and Linda Eder, Couple Who Made Music on Stage and in Studio, Split Up"] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20050906082811/http://www.playbill.com/news/article/87605.html |date=September 6, 2005 }}, playbill.com, July 28, 2004</ref> He and Eder divorced in 2004.

Wildhorn was engaged to [[Brandi Burkhardt]], best known for playing the role of Crickett on ''[[Hart of Dixie]]''. He also wrote the roles of Alice in Wonderland and Bonnie in Bonnie & Clyde for her.<ref>Kaye, Kimberly. [http://www.broadway.com/Brandi-Burkhardt/broadway_news/571286 Fresh Face: Brandi Burkhardt], broadway.com, September 18, 2008</ref> They separated in late 2010.

In 2014, he announced his engagement to [[Yoka Wao]], a former Takarazuka male-role top star who played the lead role in ''[[Never Say Goodbye (musical)|Never Say Goodbye]]'' and the Japanese production of ''Dracula''. They were married on July 26, 2015, in [[Maui]], Hawaii.<ref>Osnes, Laura. [https://instagram.com/p/5nt1_5FSra/], Instagram.com, July 26, 2015</ref>
