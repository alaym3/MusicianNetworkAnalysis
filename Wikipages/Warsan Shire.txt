'''Warsan Shire''' {{post-nominals|country=GBR|FRSL}} (born 1 August 1988) is a British writer, poet, editor and teacher, who was born to [[Somali people|Somali]] parents in [[Kenya]].<ref name=TNY>{{cite web|work=[[The New Yorker]]|url=http://www.newyorker.com/culture/cultural-comment/the-writing-life-of-a-young-prolific-poet-warsan-shire|author=Okeowo, Alexis|date=21 October 2015|title=The Writing Life of a Young, Prolific Poet}}</ref> In 2013 she was awarded the inaugural [[Brunel University African Poetry Prize]], chosen from a shortlist of six candidates out of a total 655 entries.<ref name="Spwsohapa"/> Her words "No one leaves home unless/home is the mouth of a shark", from the poem "Conversations about Home (at a deportation centre)", have been called "a rallying call for refugees and their advocates".<ref name="Kuo">{{cite news|last1=Kuo|first1=Lily|title='HOME' This poem is now the rallying call for refugees: 'No one leaves home unless home is the mouth of a shark'|url=https://qz.com/897871/warsan-shires-poem-captures-the-reality-of-life-for-refugees-no-one-leaves-home-unless-home-is-the-mouth-of-a-shark/|accessdate=1 February 2017|work=Quartz Africa|date=30 January 2017}}</ref>

==Life and career==
Born on 1 August 1988 in [[Kenya]] to Somali parents, Shire migrated with her family to the United Kingdom at the age of one. She has four siblings.<ref name="Wrse">{{cite web|title=Bio|url=http://www.warsanshire.com/|publisher=WarsanShire.com|accessdate=26 April 2015}}</ref> She has a Bachelor of Arts degree in Creative Writing. As of 2015, she primarily resides in [[Los Angeles]], California.<ref name="Wrse"/>

In 2011, she released ''Teaching My Mother How To Give Birth'', a poetry pamphlet published by [[flipped eye publishing|Flipped eye]]. Her full collection was released in 2016, also through Flipped Eye.<ref name="Wrse"/>

Shire has read her poetry in various artistic venues throughout the world, including in the United Kingdom, Italy, Germany, North America, South Africa and Kenya.<ref name="Wswbuappt">{{cite web|title=Warsan Shire Wins Brunel University African Poetry Prize 2013|author=Carolyn|url=http://bookslive.co.za/blog/2013/04/30/warsan-shire-wins-brunel-university-african-poetry-prize-2013/|work=Books Live|date=30 April 2013|accessdate=30 November 2013}}</ref> Her poems have been published in various literary publications, including ''[[Poetry Review]]'', ''Magma'' and ''[[Wasafiri]]''.<ref name="Wswbuappt"/> Additionally, Shire's verse has been featured in the collections ''Salt Book of Younger Poets'' ([[Salt Publishing|Salt]], 2011), ''Ten: The New Wave'' ([[Bloodaxe Books|Bloodaxe]], 2014), and ''[[New Daughters of Africa]]'' (edited by [[Margaret Busby]], 2019).<ref>Obi-Young, Otosirieze (10 January 2018), [https://brittlepaper.com/2018/01/anthology-edited-margaret-busby-collects-writing-200-african-women-generations/ "Margaret Busby-Edited Anthology to Feature 200 Female Writers Including Adichie, Aminatta Forna, Bernardine Evaristo, Imbolo Mbue, Warsan Shire, Zadie Smith"], ''[[Brittle Paper]]''.</ref> Her poetry has also been translated into a number of languages, including Italian, Spanish, [[Portuguese language|Portuguese]], [[Swedish language|Swedish]], [[Danish language|Danish]] and [[Estonian language|Estonian]].<ref name="Wrse"/>

As of 2016, Shire is working on her first full-length poetry collection, having put out a limited-release pamphlet called ''Her Blue Body'' in 2015.<ref>{{cite web|title=New Warsan Shire Pamphlet From Spread The Word|url=http://africanpoetrybf.unl.edu/?p=3474|publisher=African Poetry Book Fund|date=12 November 2015|accessdate=29 March 2016}}</ref> She serves as the poetry editor at ''SPOOK'' magazine and she teaches poetry workshops both globally and online for cathartic and aesthetic purposes.<ref name="Wrse"/>

Shire's poetry featured prominently in [[Beyoncé]]'s 2016 feature-length film ''[[Lemonade (Beyoncé album)|Lemonade]]''.<ref name="Lemonade">{{cite web | url=http://www.okayafrica.com/news/beyonce-lemonade-ibeyi-laolu-senbanjo-warsan-shire/ | title=Ibeyi, Laolu Senbanjo, Warsan Shire Featured in Beyoncé's 'Lemonade' | website=Okay Africa | date=23 April 2016 | accessdate=23 April 2016 | author=Leaf, Aaron}}</ref> Knowles-Carter's interest in using Shire's work was sparked with Shire's piece "For Women Who Are Difficult to Love".<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://mic.com/articles/141711/here-s-the-warsan-shire-poem-that-caught-beyonc-s-attention-for-lemonade#.KtM17Fapm|title=Here's the Warsan Shire Poem That Caught Beyoncé's Attention for 'Lemonade'|last=King|first=Jamilah|newspaper=Mic|date=25 April 2016|access-date=6 December 2016}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2016/04/28/arts/music/warsan-shire-who-gave-poetry-to-beyonces-lemonade.html|title=Warsan Shire, the Woman Who Gave Poetry to Beyoncé's 'Lemonade'|last=Hess|first=Amanda|date=27 April 2016|newspaper=The New York Times|issn=0362-4331|access-date=6 December 2016}}</ref>

== Influences ==
Shire uses not only her own personal experiences but also the experiences of people to whom she is close. She is quoted as saying: "I either know, or I am, every person I have written about, for or as. But I do imagine them in their most intimate settings."<ref name=TNY/> Her main interest is writing about and for people who are generally not heard otherwise, e.g. immigrants and refugees as well as other marginalized groups of people.<ref name=TNY/><ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.theguardian.com/books/booksblog/2016/apr/27/warsan-shire-young-poet-laureate-beyonce-lemonade-london|title=Warsan Shire: the Somali-British poet quoted by Beyoncé in Lemonade|last=Zakaria|first=Rafia|date=27 April 2016|newspaper=The Guardian|issn=0261-3077|access-date=6 December 2016}}</ref> Shire is also quoted as saying: "I also navigate a lot through memory, my memories and other people's memories, trying to essentially just make sense of stuff."<ref name="Spwsohapa">{{cite news|title=Somali poet Warsan Shire on her African poetry award|url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-africa-22358337|accessdate=30 November 2013|publisher=BBC (podcast)|date=30 April 2013}}</ref> As a first-generation immigrant, she has used her poetry to connect with her home country of Somalia, which she has never been to.<ref name="Spwsohapa" /> She uses this position as an immigrant herself to convey the lives of these peoples.<ref name=TNY/> Shire utilizes the influences of her close relatives, and family members and their experiences to depict in her poetry the struggles that they have all faced.<ref name="Spwsohapa" />

==Awards and honours==
Shire has received various awards for her art. In April 2013, she was presented with [[Brunel University]]'s inaugural [[Brunel University African Poetry Prize|African Poetry Prize]],<ref name="Spwsohapa" /> an award earmarked for poets who have yet to publish a full-length poetry collection.<ref name="Wswbuappt"/> She was chosen from a shortlist of six candidates out of a total 655 entries.<ref name="Spwsohapa"/>

In October 2013, Shire was selected from a shortlist of six as the first Young Poet Laureate for London. The honour is part of the [[London Legacy Development Corporation]]'s Spoke programme, which focuses on promoting arts and culture in [[Queen Elizabeth Olympic Park]] and the surrounding area.<ref name="Wsaalfypl">{{cite news|title=Warsan Shire announced as London's first young poet laureate|url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-england-london-24375551|accessdate=30 November 2013|publisher=BBC|date=3 October 2013}}</ref>

In 2014, Shire was also chosen as poet-in-residence of [[Queensland]], Australia, liaising with the Aboriginal Centre for Performing Arts over a six-week period.<ref name="Wrse"/>

In June 2018 Shire was elected Fellow of the [[Royal Society of Literature]] in its "40 Under 40" initiative.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.theguardian.com/books/2018/jun/28/royal-society-of-literature-40-under-40-fellows|title=Royal Society of Literature admits 40 new fellows to address historical biases|last=Flood|first=Alison|date=28 June 2018|website=The Guardian|access-date=3 July 2018}}</ref>
