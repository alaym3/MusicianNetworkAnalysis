'''Victor Mizzy''' (January 9, 1916 – October 17, 2009) was an American [[composer]] for [[television]] and [[Film|movies]] and musician whose best-known works are the themes to the 1960s television [[sitcom]]s ''[[Green Acres]]'' and ''[[The Addams Family (1964 TV series)|The Addams Family]]''.  Mizzy also wrote top-20 songs from the 1930s to 1940s.<ref name="latimes">{{cite news|title= Vic Mizzy dies at 93; film and TV composer wrote 'Addams Family' theme song |work= LA Times|date=October 20, 2009|url= https://articles.latimes.com/2009/oct/20/local/me-vic-mizzy20|access-date=2010-08-24}}</ref>

== Early life ==
Mizzy was born in [[Brooklyn, New York]], and attended [[New York University]].<ref name=var>Burlingame, Jon. "[https://www.variety.com/article/VR1118010124.html?categoryId=16&cs=1&nid=2248 Composer Vic Mizzy Dies at 93]", ''[[Variety (magazine)|Variety]]'', October 19, 2009, 3:14 p.m. PT</ref> As a child, he played accordion and piano, and was largely self-taught as a composer.<ref name=var /> During [[World War II]], he served in the [[United States Navy]] where he wrote some of his hit songs.<ref name=var />

== Songwriting ==
In the late 1930s, while based in [[New York City]],<ref name=var /> Mizzy began composing a string of popular songs.<ref>{{Cite book|last=Lentz |first=Harris M., III |year=2010 |title=Obituaries in the Performing Arts, 2009: Film, Television, Radio, Theatre, Dance, Music, Cartoons and Pop Culture |location=Jefferson, North Carolina |publisher=McFarland |pages=367–368 |isbn=978-0-7864-5645-1 }}</ref> These included [[Doris Day]]'s 1945 hit "[[My Dreams Are Getting Better All the Time]]".<ref name=var /> Other Mizzy compositions were "There's a Faraway Look in Your Eye" and "[[Three Little Sisters]]", both co-written with lyricist [[Irving Taylor (songwriter)|Irving Taylor]]. The latter were sung by [[The Andrews Sisters]] on [[Decca Records]] and in the 1942 [[Universal Pictures|Universal]] film ''[[Private Buckaroo]]'', in which the sisters appeared with [[Harry James]]' big band.

[[Dinah Shore]] also recorded "Three Little Sisters"; "Take It Easy" (also with lyricist Taylor), "Pretty Kitty Blue Eyes", "The Whole World Is Singing My Song", "Choo'n Gum" (recorded by The Andrews Sisters, as well as [[Teresa Brewer]]), "The Jones Boy", which was a 1953 hit for The [[Mills Brothers]], and "With a Hey and a Hi and a Ho-Ho-Ho".<ref name="var" />

=== Film and television ===
Mizzy broke into television circa 1959, composing music for ''[[Shirley Temple]]'s Storybook'' and the themes for ''[[Moment of Fear]]'', ''[[Klondike (TV series)|Klondike]]'' and ''[[Kentucky Jones]]''.<ref name=var /> During the 1960s, he wrote themes and scores for the hit shows ''[[Green Acres]]'' and ''[[The Addams Family (1964 TV series)|The Addams Family]]'', as well as for other [[sitcom]]s, including ''[[The Pruitts of Southampton]]'', ''[[The Double Life of Henry Phyfe]]'', ''[[Captain Nice]]'', ''[[The Don Rickles Show]]'', and ''[[Temperatures Rising]]'',<ref name=var /> and the 1964–1965 [[comedy drama]] ''[[Kentucky Jones]]''.<ref>[http://ctva.biz/US/Drama/KentuckyJones.htm Classic TV Archive Kentucky Jones (1964-65)]</ref>

A soundtrack album was released in 1965 containing all his compositions from ''The Addams Family'' entitled ''Original Music From The Addams Family''.<ref>{{cite web |title=Vic Mizzy – Original Music From The Addams Family |url=https://www.discogs.com/Vic-Mizzy-Original-Music-From-The-Addams-Family/master/411830 |website=discogs |access-date=15 October 2019}}</ref> Mizzy rewrote and conducted ''[[The Addams Family Theme]]'' with a slightly different melody for the 1977 television special ''[[Halloween with the New Addams Family]]'', which reunited most of the original cast of the 1964–1966 TV series.<ref>[https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jlw2CFiSCgc ''Halloween with the New Addams Family'' (1977), HQ, YouTube]</ref>

He also wrote the scores for five [[Don Knotts]] films, including ''[[The Ghost and Mr. Chicken]]'' (1966), ''[[The Reluctant Astronaut]]'' (1967), ''[[The Shakiest Gun in the West]]'' (1968) (released on CD by Percepto in 2007), ''[[The Love God?]]'' (1969) and ''[[How to Frame a Figg]]'' (1971),<ref name="var" /> releasing scores on a [[Compact Disc]] companion to some of their [[DVD]] releases.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.vicmizzy.com/discography.html |title=Archived copy |access-date=September 13, 2010 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110525012820/http://www.vicmizzy.com/discography.html |archive-date=May 25, 2011 }} Discography at VicMizzy.com</ref>

His other film work includes the scores for the [[William Castle]] films ''[[The Night Walker (film)|The Night Walker]]'' (1964), ''[[The Busy Body (film)|The Busy Body]]'' (1967) and ''[[The Spirit Is Willing]]'' (1967), as well as other 1960s movies such as ''[[A Very Special Favor]]'' (1965), ''[[The Caper of the Golden Bulls]]'' (1967), ''[[Don't Make Waves]]'' (1967), ''[[The Perils of Pauline (1967 film)|The Perils of Pauline]]'' (1967) and ''[[Did You Hear the One About the Traveling Saleslady?]]'' (1968).

He also composed underscores for the television series ''[[The Richard Boone Show]]'' and ''[[Quincy, M.E.]]'', as well as for such television films as ''[[The Deadly Hunt]]'' (1971), ''[[Hurricane (1974 film)|Hurricane]]'' (1974), ''Terror on the 40th Floor'' (1974), ''[[The Million Dollar Rip-Off]]'' (1976) and ''[[The Munsters' Revenge]]'' (1981).<ref name="var" /> Toward the end of his career, Mizzy was commissioned by film producer [[Sam Raimi]] to compose music for the [[blooper|outtakes]] (gag reel) special features for the DVD releases of the films ''[[Spider-Man 2]]'' and ''[[Spider-Man 3]]''.<ref>{{Cite news|last=Hale|first=Mike|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2009/10/21/arts/television/21mizzy.html|title=Vic Mizzy, Songwriter of 'Addams Family' Fame, Dies at 93|date=2009-10-21|work=The New York Times|access-date=2020-03-10|language=en-US|issn=0362-4331}}</ref>

== Family ==
Mizzy had two children with his first wife, [[Mary Small]], who as a 1930s child singer had been known as "The Little Girl With The Big Voice," and who remained popular (especially on radio) through the 1950s.{{Citation needed|date=October 2009}} One of her daughters, Patty Keeler, a singer and songwriter, often worked with songwriter [[Doc Pomus]].{{Citation needed|date=October 2009}}

== Death ==
Mizzy died at his home in [[Bel Air, Los Angeles|Bel Air, California]], on October 17, 2009, aged 93.<ref name="TAP">{{cite news|url=https://www.google.com/hostednews/ap/article/ALeqM5jj4yj37U9DWYfLMh8iNpt2MokknAD9BEHS3G2|title=Obituaries in the news|date=October 20, 2009|work=The Associated Press|access-date=October 20, 2009}}</ref> He was predeceased by a daughter who died in 1995; another daughter survives him.<ref name=var /> He was interred at [[Eden Memorial Park Cemetery|Eden Memorial Park]] in [[Mission Hills, Los Angeles|Mission Hills, California]].
