'''Freddie McGregor''' (born 27 June 1956, [[Clarendon Parish, Jamaica|Clarendon]], [[Jamaica]])<ref name="amg"/> is a [[Jamaica]]n singer, musician and record producer. His music career began when he was seven years old.<ref name="amg">{{cite web|author=Steve Huey |url=https://www.allmusic.com/artist/freddie-mcgregor-mn0000798477/biography |title=Freddie McGregor &#124; Biography & History |website=[[AllMusic]] |date=1956-06-27 |access-date=2020-04-25}}</ref>

==Biography==
In 1963 he joined with [[Ernest Wilson (singer)|Ernest Wilson]] and Peter Austin to form [[The Clarendonians]], and began to record for the [[Studio One (record label)|Studio One]] [[record label|label]].  He was only seven years old at the time and was known as 'Little Freddie'.<ref name="Jackson" /><ref name="Cooke">Cooke, Mel (2014) "[http://jamaica-gleaner.com/gleaner/20140720/ent/ent2.html Half-Century Of Freddie]", ''[[Jamaica Gleaner]]'', 20 July 2014. Retrieved 21 July 2014</ref> He was also a member of the Generation Gap.<ref name="Jackson1406">Jackson, Kevin (2014) "[http://www.jamaicaobserver.com/entertainment/52-years-of-Freddie-McGregor_16891280 52 years of Freddie McGregor]", ''[[Jamaica Observer]]'', 15 June 2014. Retrieved 15 June 2014</ref> 

McGregor converted to [[Rastafari]] in 1975.<ref name="amg"/> He is a member of the [[Mansions of Rastafari#Twelve Tribes of Israel|Twelve Tribes]] organisation.

McGregor worked with producer [[Niney the Observer]] during the late 1970s and early 1980s, and in the same period was part of the resurgence of Studio One.<ref name="Jackson1406" /> His popularity soared in the early 1980s with the release of "Bobby Babylon". Other popular [[hit record|hits]] of McGregor's include "Big Ship", "Push Comes to Shove", "Just Don't Want to Be Lonely" (a top ten hit in the UK),<ref name="Cooke" /><ref name="Jackson1406" /> and "I Was Born a Winner"; as well as [[cover version]]s of many early [[reggae]] [[pop standards|standards]]. He has also worked with producers [[Henry "Junjo" Lawes|Junjo Lawes]], [[Linval Thompson]], and [[Gussie Clarke]]. McGregor has also recorded as a drummer with artists such as [[Sugar Minott]] and [[Judy Mowatt]].<ref name="Jackson1406" />

McGregor has also toured extensively for many years. He secured a licensing agreement with [[RAS Records]] in US and released ''Come on Over'' in 1983.

McGregor's albums in the 2000s were ''Signature'' and ''Anything for You'', which received a [[Grammy Award|Grammy]] nomination.<ref name="amg"/><ref name="Jackson1406" />

He established the Big Ship label in 1983, and has produced many artists including [[Papa San]], [[Lieutenant Stitchie]], [[Tiger (musician)|Tiger]], [[Luciano (singer)|Luciano]] and Mikey Spice.<ref name="Jackson1406" />

In a 2011 interview, he expressed concern that many veteran artists, including himself, struggled for local radio play of their newer material, which he felt may be "lost" over time as a result.<ref>Campbell, Howard. [http://jamaica-gleaner.com/gleaner/20110307/ent/ent1.html Freddie approaches 50 years in music biz]. ''[[Jamaica Gleaner]]''. 7 March 2011. Retrieved 8 March 2011</ref>

His album ''Di Captain'', released in August 2012, featured [[Etana (musician)|Etana]] and [[Gappy Ranks]].<ref name="Jackson">Jackson, Kevin (2012) "[http://www.jamaicaobserver.com/Entertainment/Freddie-s-Di-Captain-sets-sail-in-August_11755918 Freddie's Di Captain sets sail in August]", ''[[Jamaica Observer]]'', 20 June 2012, retrieved 24 June 2013</ref>

In 2013, he received a Marcus Garvey Lifetime Achievement Award from the Institute of Caribbean Studies.<ref>Scott, Derrick (2013) "[http://jamaica-gleaner.com/gleaner/20131119/ent/ent3.html Marcus Garvey Lifetime Achievement Award For Freddie]", ''[[Jamaica Gleaner]]'', 19 November 2013. Retrieved 19 November 2013</ref>

One of McGregor's three sons, [[Di Genius|Stephen "Di Genius" McGregor]], is a [[dancehall]] record producer.
