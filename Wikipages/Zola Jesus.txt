'''Nika Roza Danilova''' (born '''Nicole Rose Hummel'''; April 11, 1989), known professionally by her [[stage name]] '''Zola Jesus''', is an American singer, songwriter, and record producer.<ref name="allmusic_bio">{{cite web|url=http://www.allmusic.com/artist/zola-jesus-p1141912/biography|title=Zola Jesus|publisher=AllMusic|last=Phares|first=Heather|access-date=May 9, 2020}}</ref> Her music has been noted for combining elements of [[electronic music|electronic]], [[industrial music|industrial]], [[classical music|classical]], and [[gothic rock|goth]].<ref>{{cite web |last=Orton |first=Karen |url=http://www.dazeddigital.com/music/article/11560/1/20-qas-zola-jesus |title=20 Q&As: Zola Jesus |work=[[Dazed|Dazed & Confused]] |date=October 2011 |access-date=October 15, 2011}}</ref><ref name="nme-stridulum">{{cite web |last=Hewitt |first=Ben |url=http://www.nme.com/reviews/zola-jesus/11522 |title=Album review: Zola Jesus – 'Stridulum II' |work=[[NME]] |publisher=[[IPC Media]] |date=August 23, 2010 |access-date=January 1, 2011}}</ref> Born in [[Phoenix, Arizona]], she spent her formative years in northern [[Wisconsin]], where she began writing and recording music independently while attending college. During her second year of studies, she released two singles through the independent label [[Sacred Bones Records|Sacred Bones]], followed by two [[Extended play|EPs]]. Her debut album,  ''[[The Spoils (Zola Jesus album)|The Spoils]]'', was released in 2009.

After graduating from the [[University of Wisconsin–Madison]], she released her second full-length record, ''[[Stridulum II]]'' (2010), followed by ''[[Conatus (album)|Conatus]]'' (2011). Her fourth album, ''[[Taiga (Zola Jesus album)|Taiga]]'' (2014), released by [[Mute Records]], marked a departure from her previous releases, and featured a more prominent [[pop music|pop]]-influenced sound. Her fifth studio album, ''[[Okovi]]'', was released in 2017.

==Biography==
===1989–2008: Early life and career beginnings===
Nicole Rose Hummel<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.isni.org/000000035646363X|publisher=[[International Standard Name Identifier|ISNI]]|title=ISN:000000035646363X; Danilova, Nika Roza; Hummel, Nicole Rose|archive-url=https://archive.today/20200510003549/http://www.isni.org/000000035646363X|archive-date=May 10, 2020|url-status=live}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://us-companies.info/wa/5577822_zola_jesus_inc |title=Company ZOLA JESUS INC |website=Us-companies.info |date=May 30, 2014 |access-date=August 17, 2016}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |url=http://onwisconsin.uwalumni.com/content/uploads/2014/05/summer2014.pdf |title=Class Notes |work=[[University of Wisconsin–Madison|On Winconsin]] |page=61 |year=2014 |access-date=August 17, 2016}}</ref> was born April 11, 1989,<ref name="allmusic_bio"/>{{unreliable source?|date=July 2020}} in [[Phoenix, Arizona]], where she spent her early years.<ref>{{cite news|last=Farah |first=Troy |url=http://www.phoenixnewtimes.com/music/zola-jesus-resented-her-parents-for-moving-her-from-phoenix-6609728 |title=Zola Jesus Resented Her Parents for Moving Her From Phoenix |newspaper=[[Phoenix New Times]] |date=February 6, 2015|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20151218221621/http://www.phoenixnewtimes.com/music/zola-jesus-resented-her-parents-for-moving-her-from-phoenix-6609728|archive-date=December 18, 2015}}</ref> Her parents are first-generation Americans,<ref name="save"/> with combinations of [[Russia]]n as well as [[Germans|German]],<ref name="pitchfork-interview">{{cite web |last=Pelly |first=Jenn |url=https://pitchfork.com/features/update/9455-zola-jesus/ |title=Zola Jesus |work=[[Pitchfork (website)|Pitchfork]] |date=July 29, 2014 |access-date=August 9, 2016}}</ref> [[Slovenia]]n,<ref name="timeout-article">{{cite web |url=https://www.timeout.com/newyork/nightlife/zola-jesus-presents-her-after-the-fall-of-new-york-mix
|title= Zola Jesus presents her 'After the Fall of New York' mix
|access-date=August 9, 2016}}</ref> and [[Ukrainians|Ukrainian]] descent.<ref name="save"/> Her grandparents immigrated to the United States from [[Odessa]], Ukraine, and settled in [[North Dakota]].<ref name=save>{{cite web|url=https://www.elle.com/culture/music/news/a24991/zola-jesus-interview/|work=[[Elle (magazine)|Elle]]|title=Zola Jesus Wants to Change the World|date=July 16, 2014|author=Kletnoy, Sergio|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150119041742/https://www.elle.com/culture/music/news/a24991/zola-jesus-interview/|archive-date=January 19, 2015}}</ref> Honoring her ancestry, she formally goes by her [[Slavic languages|Slavic]] name, Nika Roza Danilova.<ref name=arena>{{cite web|url=https://arena.com/article/zola-jesus-okovi-review|title=Zola Jesus Returns Home on Her New Goth-Pop Album|work=[[Arena (Swedish magazine)|Arena]]|last=Sanna|first=Jacopo|url-status=live|archive-url=https://archive.today/20200510003102/https://arena.com/article/zola-jesus-okovi-review|archive-date=May 10, 2020|date=October 13, 2017}}</ref> She has one older brother.<ref name=save/>

During her childhood, she and her family relocated to [[Merrill, Wisconsin]], where they lived on a rural property situated in {{convert|100|acre|ha}} of forest.<ref name="save"/> "I resented my parents for having moved me away from Phoenix because I thought Phoenix was much more interesting than Wisconsin," she said. "It's a great place and it's very interesting there, beautiful."<ref>{{cite news|work=[[Phoenix New Times]]|title=Zola Jesus Resented Her Parents for Moving Her From Phoenix|date=February 6, 2015|author=Farah, Troy|access-date=June 9, 2019|url=https://www.phoenixnewtimes.com/music/zola-jesus-resented-her-parents-for-moving-her-from-phoenix-6609728}}</ref>

She attended Wausau West High School, and after, enrolled at the [[University of Wisconsin–Milwaukee]] to study business.<ref name="allmusic_bio"/> She began recording music at home while still in college, using keyboards, drum machines and other instruments. Her performing name, Zola Jesus, is a compound derived from the French writer [[Émile Zola]], and the [[Jesus|Christian messiah]].<ref>{{cite web|work=[[The Independent]]|title=Zola Jesus: 'I feel like I have to push myself off a cliff'|date=November 20, 2011|last=Montgomery|first=Hugh|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20111223152353/http://www.independent.co.uk/arts-entertainment/music/features/zola-jesus-i-feel-like-i-have-to-push-myself-off-a-cliff-6264808.html|url=http://www.independent.co.uk/arts-entertainment/music/features/zola-jesus-i-feel-like-i-have-to-push-myself-off-a-cliff-6264808.html|archive-date=December 23, 2011}}</ref>

While studying at the University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee, she debuted the singles "Poor Sons" on Die Stasi and "Soeur Sewer", released in 2008 by Sacred Bones Records,<ref name="allmusic_bio"/> after which she transferred to the [[University of Wisconsin–Madison]].<ref name="expressmilwaukee">{{cite web
| author = Rytlewski, Evan| url = http://www.expressmilwaukee.com/article-10066-zola-jesusrs-nika-roza-danilova-talks-opera-apocalypse.html| title = Zola Jesus' Nika Roza Danilova Talks Opera, Apocalypse| work = Express Milwaukee| access-date = January 11, 2011}}</ref> In 2009, while still in college, she recorded her debut full-length ''[[The Spoils (Zola Jesus album)|The Spoils]]'', released by Sacred Bones in July 2009.<ref name="pitchfork_int">{{cite web|url=http://pitchfork.com/news/37242-rising-zola-jesus/|title=Rising: Zola Jesus – interview|work=[[Pitchfork (website)|Pitchfork]]|author=Dombal, Ryan|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171009144437/https://pitchfork.com/news/37242-rising-zola-jesus/|archive-date=October 9, 2017|date=December 1, 2009}}</ref> In 2010, she graduated from the University of Wisconsin–Madison<ref name="UW–Madison Weekly">{{cite web |url=http://theweekly.wisc.edu/newsletter/badgers-in-the-world-the-weekly/new-post-for-badgers-in-the-world-the-weekly-17/ |title=UW grad impresses with new Zola Jesus album |last1=Kirkby |first1=Sean |date=October 23, 2013 |work=The Weekly |publisher=[[University of Wisconsin–Madison]] |archive-url=https://www.webcitation.org/6KdlAqYYa?url=http://theweekly.wisc.edu/newsletter/badgers-in-the-world-the-weekly/new-post-for-badgers-in-the-world-the-weekly-17/ |archive-date=October 25, 2013 |url-status=live }}</ref> with a double major in [[French language|French]] and [[philosophy]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.sfweekly.com/music/zola-jesus-sings-about-the-end-of-the-world-hates-upbeat-indie-rock/|work=[[SF Weekly]]|title=Zola Jesus Sings About the End of the World, Hates Upbeat Indie Rock|date=May 4, 2011|last=Palermo|first=Tomas|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190613005605/http://www.sfweekly.com/music/zola-jesus-sings-about-the-end-of-the-world-hates-upbeat-indie-rock/|url-status=live|archive-date=June 13, 2019}}</ref>

Danilova followed these three EPs with her second full-length album, ''[[Stridulum II]]'', which was released in August 2010, one month after ''LA Vampires Meets Zola Jesus''. This album marked her feature full-length release in the United Kingdom, combining all six songs from the ''Stridulum'' EP (in a different sequence), along with three of the four tracks on the ''Valusia'' EP; the cover artwork is a slightly-altered version of the artwork from the ''Stridulum'' EP.<ref name=snapes>{{cite web|url=https://www.nme.com/reviews/zola-jesus/11522|title=Album review: Zola Jesus – 'Stridulum II'|work=[[NME]]|date=August 23, 2010|access-date=May 4, 2019|last=Snapes|first=Laura|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100827180852/http://www.nme.com/reviews/zola-jesus/11522|archive-date=August 27, 2010|url-status=dead}}</ref> Laura Snapes of ''[[NME]]'' praised the album as a "gorgeously ethereal soundscape of a thousand years of heartbreak unleashed into one might howl."<ref name=snapes/> Additionally, ''NME'' ranked it the seventh-best album of the year.<ref name=richards/>

In 2011, Danilova began composing her third full-length studio album, ''[[Conatus (album)|Conatus]]'', which she co-produced with Brian Foote of [[Jackie-O Motherfucker]] and [[Cloudland Canyon]].<ref name=young/> The album, released in September 2011 and named for the [[Latin]] word for "moving forward," featured less electronic components than Danilova's previous records, and included cello, double bass, violin, and viola.<ref name=young>{{cite web|work=[[Consequence of Sound]]|date=June 29, 2011|last=Young|first=Alex|url=https://consequenceofsound.net/2011/06/zola-jesus-announces-new-album-conatus/|title=Zola Jesus announces new album: Conatus|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140823150122/https://consequenceofsound.net/2011/06/zola-jesus-announces-new-album-conatus/|archive-date=August 23, 2014}}</ref> "I just wanted to try out new ways to produce and different ways to write," Danilova commented. "And with this new record it's a lot more stripped down and there are way more acoustic elements as opposed to no acoustic elements&nbsp;... I just wanted more breathing room, so people could insert their own experiences into the song, their own feelings into the song, as opposed to me forcing it on them with the densest sound possible."<ref name=bosman>{{cite web |last=Bosman |first=Chris |url=http://www.prefixmag.com/features/pitchfork-music-festival-zola-jesus/zola-jesus-pitchfork-festival-interview/54440/ |title=Zola Jesus: Interview At Pitchfork Music Festival 2011 |work=Prefix |date=July 19, 2011|url-status=live|archive-url=https://archive.today/20130131183241/http://www.prefixmag.com/features/pitchfork-music-festival-zola-jesus/zola-jesus-pitchfork-festival-interview/54440/|archive-date=January 31, 2013}}</ref> Additionally, Danilova provided guest vocals on the song "Intro" by [[M83 (band)|M83]] from their 2011 album ''[[Hurry Up, We're Dreaming]]''. She also sang on "New France" by [[Orbital (band)|Orbital]], from their 2012 album ''[[Wonky (album)|Wonky]]''.

===2013–present: ''Taiga'' and ''Okovi''===
[[File:1 Zola-Jesus Webster.jpg|thumb|right|upright=.9|Zola Jesus performing at Webster Hall, New York City, 2014]]
In August 2013, ''[[Versions (Zola Jesus album)|Versions]]'', a set of neo-classical reworkings of previous tracks from Zola Jesus releases, in a collaboration with producer [[JG Thirlwell]], was released through Sacred Bones.<ref>{{cite web|work=[[The A.V. Club]]|url=https://music.avclub.com/zola-jesus-versions-1798177722|date=August 20, 2013|author=Lewis, Catherine|title=Zola Jesus: ''Versions''|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20191122104552/https://music.avclub.com/zola-jesus-versions-1798177722|archive-date=November 22, 2019}}</ref>

After having resided in [[Los Angeles]] for a period, Danilova found living in a large city was negatively impacting her ability to concentrate, and relocated to [[Vermont]], followed by her native Wisconsin, before settling in [[Washington (state)|Washington]] to begin completing her new record.<ref name=garber/> On June 18, 2014, she announced the release of her fourth studio album, ''[[Taiga (Zola Jesus album)|Taiga]]''.<ref name="taiga-new">{{cite web | url=http://pitchfork.com/news/55621-zola-jesus-announces-new-album-taiga/ | title=Zola Jesus Announces New Album Taiga | work= Pitchfork | date=June 18, 2014 |url-status=live| archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20190417124703/https://pitchfork.com/news/55621-zola-jesus-announces-new-album-taiga/|archive-date=April 17, 2019| author=Pelly, Jenn}}</ref> This album, released by [[Mute Records]], features a more prominent [[pop music|pop]] influence than her previous releases, with Danilova drawing inspiration from popular vocalists such as [[Mariah Carey]] and [[Barbra Streisand]].<ref name=garber>{{cite web|url=https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-news/inside-zola-jesus-surprising-new-pop-album-taiga-249548/|work=[[Rolling Stone]]|title=Inside Zola Jesus’ Surprising New Pop Album ‘Taiga’|last=Garber-Paul|first=Elisabeth|date=July 16, 2014|url-status=live|archive-date=May 10, 2020|archive-url=https://archive.today/20200510004658/https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-news/inside-zola-jesus-surprising-new-pop-album-taiga-249548/}}</ref> "I was a child of the Nineties," she commented. "I grew up on [[Britney Spears]], the [[Spice Girls]], and I was listening to a lot of vocal music. You know, divas — Mariah Carey, [[Aaliyah]], Barbra Streisand — strong female vocals. [On this record] I wanted to be really confident in my voice, which is usually very intense."<ref name=garber/>

In 2017, Danilova relocated to her hometown of Merrill, Wisconsin, and built a home on her family's property.<ref name=pelly>{{cite web|work=Pitchfork|url=https://pitchfork.com/thepitch/zola-jesus-new-album-okovi-interview/|title=Zola Jesus Turns Tragedy into a Torrential Sound on Her New Album|date=July 21, 2017|access-date=March 5, 2018|author=Pelly, Jenn}}</ref> In September 2017, she returned to Sacred Bones to release her fifth studio album, ''[[Okovi]]'', which she wrote after a protracted bout of depression.<ref name=hilton>{{cite web|url=https://www.npr.org/sections/allsongs/2017/09/08/549008336/zola-jesus-reveals-the-dark-remarkable-stories-behind-her-new-album-okovi|work=[[NPR]]|date=September 8, 2017|title=Zola Jesus Reveals The Dark, Remarkable Stories Behind Her New Album, 'Okovi'|last=Hilton|first=Robin|url-status=live|archive-url=https://archive.today/20200510022408/https://www.npr.org/sections/allsongs/2017/09/08/549008336/zola-jesus-reveals-the-dark-remarkable-stories-behind-her-new-album-okovi|archive-date=May 10, 2020}}</ref> Prior to composing the album, Danilova said she spent time "watching movies, reading books, then I moved back to Wisconsin, started building my house, but mostly I was just trying to give myself time to live, because you write an album, then you tour and you forget you are a human being, you become a gipsy in a way, so it has been important to me to give myself more time to live outside of the music."<ref name=collini/> The album marked her first to feature [[guitar]] instrumentation.<ref name=hilton/>

In May 2020, Danilova announced the release of her first live album, ''[[Live at Roadburn 2018 (Zola Jesus album)|Live at Roadburn 2018]]'', which features 12 live tracks from the 2018 [[Roadburn Festival]] in the Netherlands. It was initially made available via [[Bandcamp]], and scheduled for a CD and double-LP release in June 2020.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://consequenceofsound.net/2020/05/zola-jesus-shares-new-live-album-roadburn-2018-stream/|work=Consequence of Sound|title=Zola Jesus Shares New Live Album Roadburn 2018|author=Graves, Wren|date=May 4, 2020|url-status=live|archive-url=https://archive.today/20200510043010/https://consequenceofsound.net/2020/05/zola-jesus-shares-new-live-album-roadburn-2018-stream/|archive-date=May 10, 2020}}</ref>

In 2021, Danilova was reported to be appearing on the podcast ''[[Storybound (podcast)|Storybound]]''.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://lithub.com/announcing-season-4-of-the-storybound-podcast/ |title= Announcing Season 4 of the Storybound Podcast  |accessdate= June 24, 2021}}</ref>

==Artistry==
===Influences===
Danilova has stated that she has been influenced by a number of singers and bands, including [[Ian Curtis]],<ref name="pitchfork_int"/> [[Lydia Lunch]],<ref name="pitchfork_int"/> [[Diamanda Galás]], [[Throbbing Gristle]], [[Swans (band)|Swans]]<ref name="rcrdlbl">{{cite web| url    =http://rcrdlbl.com/artists/Zola_Jesus/music| title    = Zola Jesus Music| website = RCRD LBL|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20091219063117/http://rcrdlbl.com/artists/Zola_Jesus/music|archive-date=December 19, 2009}}</ref> and [[Cristina (singer)|Cristina]].<ref name="billboard">{{cite web| url    =https://www.billboard.com/articles/columns/pop/9348835/cristina-monet-zilkha-dead-61|title=Cristina Monet Zilkha, '80s Left-Field Pop Great, Dies at 61|last=Unterberger|first=Andrew|date=April 1, 2020|work=[[Billboard (magazine)|Billboard]]}}</ref>  On her fourth album, ''Taiga'', she mentioned mainstream pop artists such as Mariah Carey and Barbra Streisand as being influences on her vocal performance.<ref name=garber/>

===Vocals and musical style===
Her style has been described variously as "commanded by ominous lyrics and a sultry Goth delivery,"<ref name="spinner_int"/> According to the ''NME'', Zola Jesus "wails like [[Kate Bush]]" on a music sometimes evoking [[Joy Division]].<ref name=richards>{{cite web|author=Richards, Sam|url=http://www.nme.com/list/50-best-albums-of-2010/199283/article/199596|title=50 Best Albums of 2010: #7: Zola Jesus ''Stridulum II''|work=NME|archive-date=December 20, 2010|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20101220020108/http://www.nme.com/list/50-best-albums-of-2010/199283/article/199596|url-status=live}}</ref> ''[[Q (magazine)|Q]]'' magazine noted her for her "haunting vocals and swirling, electronic atmospherics...&nbsp; located midway between [[Florence Welch]] and [[Siouxsie and the Banshees]]."<ref name="q_10_new_faces">''Cottingham, Chris''. Q magazine. #294 January 2011. The 10 New Faces of 2011.
