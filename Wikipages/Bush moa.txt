'''''Anomalopteryx didiformis''''' is an extinct [[flightless bird]] colloquially known as the '''lesser moa''', '''little bush moa''', or '''bush moa'''. It was the smallest known species of moa, only slightly taller than a [[Turkey (bird)|turkey]]. A slender bird, it weighed around {{convert|30|kg|lb}}.<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|title=Little bush moa {{!}} New Zealand Birds Online|url=http://nzbirdsonline.org.nz/species/little-bush-moa|access-date=2021-02-19|website=nzbirdsonline.org.nz}}</ref> It inhabited much of the [[North Island]] and small sections of the [[South Island]] of [[New Zealand]]. It [[habitat|habitated]] the dense lowland conifer, broad-leafed [[Nothofagus|Southern beech]] forests and [[Shrubland|scrubland]].<ref name=":0" /><ref name="Davies" />

[[File:Anomalopteryx didiformis.jpg|thumb|left|upright|Skeleton]] 
It possessed a sturdy, sharp-edged beak, suggesting that its diet was made up of [[Twig|twigs]] and other tough plant material.<ref name=":0" />

Native predators included the [[Haast's eagle]] and [[Eyles's harrier|Eyles' harrier]].<ref name=":0" /> The species went extinct alongside other native New Zealand wildlife around 500-600 years ago, following the arrival and proliferation of the [[Maori people]] in [[New Zealand]], as well as the introduction of [[Polynesian Dog|Polynesian dogs]].<ref name=":0" />  

As with all moa, they have with a [[sternum]] without a keel. They also have a distinctive [[palate]].<ref name="Davies">Davies, S. J. J. F. (2003)</ref> The most complete remains, a partially articulated skeleton with substantial mummified tissue and feathers were discovered in 1980 in Lake Echo Valley, east of [[Te Anau]], [[Southland, New Zealand|Southland]].<ref>{{cite journal |last=Forrest |first=R. M. |date=1987 |title=A partially mummified skeleton of ''Anomalopteryx didiformis'' from Southland. |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=MK84AAAAIAAJ&pg=PA399 |journal=Journal of the Royal Society of New Zealand |publisher=[[Royal Society of New Zealand]] |volume=17 |issue=4 |pages=399–408 |access-date=8 July 2015 |doi=10.1080/03036758.1987.10426481|doi-access=free }}</ref> It is now in the [[Southland Museum and Art Gallery]], in [[Invercargill]]. Anomalopteryx fossils made up the bulk of moa fossils discovered in a swamp in 1912 in [[Clevedon, New Zealand|Clevedon]].<ref>{{Cite journal| issn = 1174-9202| volume = 40| pages = 39–53| last = Gill| first = B.J.| title = HISTORICAL MOA BONE COLLECTIONS (AVES: DINORNITHIFORMES) AT AUCKLAND MUSEUM—CLEVEDON AND KIA ORA| journal = [[Auckland War Memorial Museum|Records of the Auckland Museum]]| date = 2003}}</ref>

Scientists at [[Harvard University]] assembled the first nearly complete genome of the species from toe bones.<ref name=":1">{{Cite web|date=2018-02-27|title=Scientists reconstruct the genome of a moa, a bird extinct for 700 years|url=https://www.statnews.com/2018/02/27/moa-extinct-bird-genome/|access-date=2021-02-20|website=STAT|language=en-US}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web | url=https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/genome/124?genome_assembly_id=605838 |title = Anomalopteryx didiformis (ID 124) - Genome - NCBI}}</ref> Thus bringing the species a step closer to being "[[De-extinction|resurrected]]" in the future.<ref name=":1" />
