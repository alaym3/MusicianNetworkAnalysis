[[File:Mattak.jpg|thumb|Sliced and prepared muktuk]]
'''Muktuk'''<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.livingdictionary.com/search/viewResults.jsp?language=en&searchString=Muktuk&languageSet=all |title=muktuk|work=Asuilaak Living Dictionary}}</ref> is a traditional [[Inuit]] and [[Chukchi people|Chukchi]] food consisting of frozen [[whale]] skin and [[blubber]].

[[File:Bowheads42.jpg|thumb|A bowhead whale, which is most often used in muktuk]]
Muktuk is most often made from the skin and blubber of the [[bowhead whale]], although the [[Beluga (whale)|beluga]] and the [[narwhal]] are also used. Usually eaten raw, today it is occasionally finely diced, breaded, deep fried, and then served with [[soy sauce]]. Despite it being usually eaten raw it could also be eaten frozen or cooked.<ref>{{Cite book|title=The A to Z of the Inuit|last=Stern|first=Pamela|publisher=Scarecrow Press|year=2009|isbn=978-0-8108-6822-9|location=Lanham|pages=101}}</ref> It is also sometimes [[pickling|pickled]].<ref>{{cite web |url=http://news.travel.aol.comna/2010/09/09/10-weirdest-foods-in-the-world/ |title=10 Weirdest Foods in the World |publisher=News.travel.aol.com |date=9 September 2010 |access-date=11 September 2013 }}{{dead link|date=February 2018 |bot=InternetArchiveBot |fix-attempted=yes }}</ref>
When chewed raw, the blubber becomes oily, with a nutty taste; if not diced, or at least serrated, the skin is quite rubbery.

In Greenland, muktuk (mattak) is sold commercially to fish factories,<ref name="heide1994">{{Cite journal |last=Heide-Jørgensen |first=Mads Peter |date=January 1994 |title=Distribution, exploitation and population status of white whales (''Delphinapterus leucas'') and narwhals (''Monodon monoceros'') in West Greenland |url=https://www.researchgate.net/publication/285286297 |journal=Meddelelser om Grønland, Bioscience |volume=39 |pages=135–149}}</ref> and in Canada to other communities (muktaaq).<ref name="hoover">{{Cite journal |authors=Hoover C, Bailey M, Higdon J, Ferguson SH, Sumalia R |date=March 2013 |title=Estimating the Economic Value of Narwhal and Beluga Hunts in Hudson Bay, Nunavut |url=https://arctic.journalhosting.ucalgary.ca/arctic/index.php/arctic/article/view/4261/4242 |journal=The Arctic Institute of North America |volume=66 |pages=1–16}}</ref>

[[File:Maktaaq 2 2002-08-10.jpg|thumb|Elders sharing maktaaq, 2002]]
[[File:Maktaaq Feast 1 1997-05-07.jpg|thumb|A group of people eating muktuk, 1997]]
Muktuk has been found to be a good source of [[vitamin C]], the [[epidermis (zoology)|epidermis]] containing up to 38&nbsp;mg per {{convert|100|g|oz}}.<ref name="arctic">{{cite journal|author1=Geraci, Joseph R. |author2=Smith, Thomas G. |name-list-style=amp |year=1979|title=Vitamin C in the Diet of Inuit Hunters From Holman, Northwest Territories|journal= Arctic|volume =32|issue=2|pages=135–139|url=http://pubs.aina.ucalgary.ca/arctic/Arctic32-2-135.pdf|jstor=40508955|doi=10.14430/arctic2611}}</ref><ref name="vitaminc">{{Cite journal | last1 = Fediuk | first1 = K. | last2 = Hidiroglou | first2 = N. | last3 = Madère | first3 = R. | last4 = Kuhnlein | first4 = H. V. | title = Vitamin C in Inuit Traditional Food and Women's Diets | doi = 10.1006/jfca.2002.1053 | journal = Journal of Food Composition and Analysis | volume = 15 | issue = 3 | pages = 221 | year = 2002 }}</ref> It was used as an [[antiscorbutic]] by British Arctic explorers.<ref>{{Citation|last=McClintock|first=Francis Leopold|title=CHAPTER XVI|work=A Narrative of the Discovery of the Fate of Sir John Franklin and His Companions|year=2012|pages=301–322|publisher=Cambridge University Press|doi=10.1017/cbo9781139236522.018|isbn=978-1-139-23652-2}}</ref> Blubber is also a source of [[vitamin D]].<ref>{{Cite journal | last1 = Kuhnlein | first1 = H. V. | last2 = Barthet | first2 = V. | last3 = Farren | first3 = A. | last4 = Falahi | first4 = E. | last5 = Leggee | first5 = D. | last6 = Receveur | first6 = O. | last7 = Berti | first7 = P. | doi = 10.1016/j.jfca.2005.02.007 | title = Vitamins A, D, and E in Canadian Arctic traditional food and adult diets | journal = Journal of Food Composition and Analysis | volume = 19 | issue = 6–7 | pages = 495 | year = 2006 }}</ref> 

As whales grow, mercury accumulates in the liver, kidney, muscle, and blubber, and cadmium settles in the blubber.<ref name="snow1983">{{cite journal|last1=Wagemann|first1=R.|last2=Snow|first2=N.B.|last3=Lutz|first3=A.|last4=Scott|first4=D.P.|year=1983 |title=Heavy Metals in Tissues and Organs of the Narwhal (''Monodon monoceras'')|journal=Canadian Journal of Fisheries and Aquatic Sciences|volume=40|issue=S2|pages=s206–s214|doi=10.1139/f83-326}}</ref> It also contains [[Polychlorinated biphenyl|PCB]]s, carcinogens that damage human nervous, immune and reproductive systems, bioaccumulated from the marine food web,<ref>{{cite web | url= https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2005/02/050213132247.htm | title=Chemical Compounds Found In Whale Blubber Are From Natural Sources, Not Industrial Contamination|date=18 February 2005
}}</ref><ref>{{cite news | url=http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/science/nature/1132889.stm | title=Japan warned on 'contaminated' blubber | work=BBC News | date=24 January 2001 | access-date=31 December 2009}}</ref> and a variety of other contaminants.<ref>{{Cite web |url=https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=bioaccumulation+narwhal |title=Google Scholar |website=scholar.google.com |access-date=2018-11-18}}</ref>
