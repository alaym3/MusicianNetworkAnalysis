'''James Edward Ingram''' (February 16, 1952 – January 29, 2019)<ref>{{cite web|title=Music Makers James Ingram|url=http://www.thehistorymakers.com/biography/james-ingram-41|publisher=The HistoryMakers|access-date=January 4, 2013}}</ref><ref name="thrdeath">{{cite web|title=James Ingram, Grammy-Winning R&B Singer, Dies at 66|url=https://www.hollywoodreporter.com/news/james-ingram-dead-grammy-winning-r-b-singer-was-66-1180817|website=The Hollywood Reporter|access-date=January 29, 2019|language=en}}</ref> was an American singer, songwriter, record producer, and instrumentalist. He was a two-time [[Grammy Award]]-winner and a two-time [[Academy Awards|Academy Award]] nominee for [[Academy Award for Best Original Song|Best Original Song]]. After beginning his career in 1973, Ingram charted eight [[Top 40]] hits on the U.S. [[Billboard Hot 100|''Billboard'' Hot 100]] chart from the early [[1980s in music|1980s]] until the early [[1990s in music|1990s]], as well as thirteen top 40 hits on the [[Hot R&B/Hip-Hop Songs|Hot R&B/Hip-Hop Songs chart]]. In addition, he charted 20 hits on the [[Adult Contemporary (chart)|Adult Contemporary chart]] (including two number-ones). He had two number-one singles on the Hot 100: the first, a [[duet]] with fellow [[R&B]] artist [[Patti Austin]], 1982's "[[Baby, Come to Me (Patti Austin and James Ingram song)|Baby, Come to Me]]" topped the U.S. pop chart in [[List of Hot 100 number-one singles of 1983 (U.S.)|1983]]; "[[I Don't Have the Heart]]", which became his second number-one in [[List of Hot 100 number-one singles of 1990 (U.S.)|1990]] was his only number-one as a solo artist.

In between these hits, he also recorded the song "[[Somewhere Out There (James Horner song)|Somewhere Out There]]" with fellow recording artist [[Linda Ronstadt]] for the animated film ''[[An American Tail]]''. The song and the music video both became gigantic hits. Ingram co-wrote "The Day I Fall in Love", from the motion picture ''[[Beethoven's 2nd (film)|Beethoven's 2nd]]'' (1993), and singer [[Patty Smyth]]'s "Look What Love Has Done", from the motion picture ''[[Junior (1994 film)|Junior]]'' (1994), which earned him nominations for Best Original Song from the [[Academy Awards|Oscars]], [[Golden Globe Award|Golden Globes]], and Grammy Awards in 1994 and 1995.

==Biography==
===Early life===
Ingram was born in [[Akron, Ohio]], where he attended Akron's [[East Community Learning Center|East High School]] and received a track scholarship to the [[University of Akron]].<ref>{{cite news|first=Malcolm|last=Abram|title=Akron-born singer James Ingram dies at 66|url=https://www.ohio.com/entertainmentlife/20190129/akron-born-singer-james-ingram-dies-at-67|work=[[Akron Beacon Journal]]|date=January 29, 2019|access-date=January 30, 2019}}</ref> Subsequently, he moved to Los Angeles and played with the band Revelation Funk, which made an appearance in the [[Rudy Ray Moore]] film ''[[Dolemite]]''. He also later played keyboards for [[Ray Charles]] before becoming famous. James Ingram received his first publishing deal with 20th Century Fox publishing company, which is where he sang the $50 demo for "Just Once".<ref name="James Ingram obituary">{{cite web|last1=Sweeting|first1=Adam|title=James Ingram obituary|url=https://www.theguardian.com/music/2019/jan/30/james-ingram-obituary|website=[[The Guardian]]|access-date=February 2, 2019|date=30 January 2019}}</ref>

===Career===
Ingram provided the vocals to "[[Just Once]]"<ref name="Just Once">{{cite web|title=Just Once|url=http://www.musicvf.com/song.php?id=33851|website=Music VF|access-date=February 2, 2019}}</ref> and "[[One Hundred Ways]]"<ref>{{cite book|title=Joel Whitburn's Top Pop Singles 1955-1990|isbn=0-89820-089-X|last1=Whitburn|first1=Joel|date=June 1991}}</ref> on [[Quincy Jones]]'s 1981 album ''[[The Dude (Quincy Jones album)|The Dude]]'', which earned Ingram triple [[Grammy]] nominations, including [[Grammy Award for Best New Artist|Best New Artist]].<ref name="The 24th Annual Grammy Awards"  >{{cite web|title=The 24th Annual Grammy Awards|url=https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0355159/|website=IMDb|access-date=February 2, 2019}}</ref> "One Hundred Ways" won him the [[Grammy Award for Best Male R&B Vocal Performance]] for his work. On December 11, 1981, Ingram appeared as a guest on the Canadian comedy series ''[[Second City Television|SCTV]]'' (which aired on [[NBC]]), singing "Just Once".<ref>{{cite web|url=http://sctvguide.ca/episodes/sctv_s42.htm#Show_6|title=The Godfather with James Ingram and John Marley|website=SCTV Series 4 Cycle 2|access-date=December 5, 2014}}</ref> Ingram's debut album, ''It's Your Night'', was released in 1983 and included the ballad "There's No Easy Way".<ref name="James Ingram obituary" /> He worked with other notable artists such as [[Donna Summer]], [[Ray Charles]], [[Anita Baker]], [[Viktor Lazlo]], [[Nancy Wilson (singer)|Nancy Wilson]], [[Natalie Cole]], and [[Kenny Rogers]]. In October 1990, he scored a No. 1 hit on the [[Billboard Hot 100|''Billboard'' Hot 100]] with the love ballad "[[I Don't Have the Heart]]",<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.billboard.com/charts/1990-10-20/hot-100|title=The Hot 100 : Oct 20, 1990 &#124; Billboard Chart Archive|website=Billboard|access-date=April 30, 2014}}</ref> from his ''It's Real'' album.

In 1984, Ingram received three additional [[Grammy]] nominations: "How Do You Keep the Music Playing?" (his second duet with recording artist [[Patti Austin]]), for Best Pop Performance by a Duo or Group with Vocals; the [[Top 40|US Top 10]] single, "[[P.Y.T. (Pretty Young Thing)]]" for [[Michael Jackson]], which Ingram and [[Quincy Jones]] co-wrote, for [[Best R&B Song]]; and the track "Party Animal" for [[Best Male R&B Vocal Performance]]. In early 1985, he was nominated for his debut album (''[[It's Your Night]]'') for Best Male R&B Vocal Performance, and for its single, "[[Yah Mo B There]]" (a duet with fellow [[R&B]] musician [[Michael McDonald (singer)|Michael McDonald]]), for Best R&B Song and Best R&B Performance by a Duo or Group, and won the latter.<ref name="James Ingram obituary" /><ref name="James Ingram, R&B star and Michael Jackson collaborator, dies aged 66">{{cite web|last1=Snapes|first1=Laura|title=James Ingram, R&B star and Michael Jackson collaborator, dies aged 66|url=https://www.theguardian.com/music/2019/jan/30/james-ingram-r-and-b-star-michael-jackson-collaborator-dies-aged-66|website=The Guardian|access-date=February 2, 2019}}</ref><ref name="US R&B singer-songwriter James Ingram dies aged 66">{{cite web|title=US R&B singer-songwriter James Ingram dies aged 66|url=https://www.bbc.com/news/entertainment-arts-47052136|website=[[BBC News]]|access-date=February 2, 2019}}</ref>

Ingram is perhaps best known for his hit collaborations with other vocalists. He scored a No. 1 hit on the Hot 100 chart in February 1983 with Patti Austin on the duet "Baby, Come to Me",<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.billboard.com/charts/1983-02-19/hot-100|title=The Hot 100 : Feb 19, 1983 &#124; Billboard Chart Archive|website=Billboard|access-date=April 30, 2014}}</ref> a song made popular on TV's ''[[General Hospital]]''. A second Austin–Ingram duet, "How Do You Keep the Music Playing?", was featured in the movie ''[[Best Friends (1982 film)|Best Friends]]'' (1982) and earned an Oscar nomination.<ref name="The 55th Academy Awards (1983) Nominees and Winners">{{cite web|title=The 55th Academy Awards (1983) Nominees and Winners|url=http://www.oscars.org/awards/academyawards/legacy/ceremony/55th-winners.html#selection-511.0-511.51|archive-url=https://archive.today/20120905211922/http://www.oscars.org/awards/academyawards/legacy/ceremony/55th-winners.html#selection-511.0-511.51|url-status=dead|archive-date=September 5, 2012|website=AMPAS|access-date=February 2, 2019}}</ref> In 1984, he teamed up with [[Kenny Rogers]] and [[Kim Carnes]] for the Top 40 ballad "What About Me?"<ref name="James Ingram obituary" /> In 1985, Ingram won a [[Grammy Awards of 1985|Grammy Award]] for "Yah Mo B There", a duet with Michael McDonald, and participated in the charity project "[[We Are the World]]".<ref name="James Ingram obituary" />

Ingram teamed with American vocalist [[Linda Ronstadt]] and had a million-selling #2 hit in the U.S. and a Top 10 U.K. hit in 1987<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.billboard.com/charts/1987-03-14/hot-100|title=The Hot 100 : Mar 14, 1987 &#124; Billboard Chart Archive|website=Billboard|date=March 14, 1987|access-date=April 30, 2014}}</ref> with "[[Somewhere Out There (James Horner song)|Somewhere Out There]]", the theme from the animated feature film ''[[An American Tail]]''. The song was awarded the 1987 [[Grammy Award]] for [[Grammy Award for Song of the Year|Song of the Year]]. It also received [[59th Academy Awards|Academy Award]] and [[44th Golden Globe Awards|Golden Globe]] nominations. It was one of the last million-selling Gold-certified 45 RPM singles to be issued by the [[RIAA]].<ref name="Linda Ronstadt - Chart History">{{cite web|title=Linda Ronstadt – Chart History|url=https://www.billboard.com/music/Linda-Ronstadt/chart-history/hot-100|website=Billboard|access-date=February 2, 2019}}</ref><ref name="The 59th Academy Awards - 1987">{{cite web|title=The 59th Academy Awards – 1987|url=https://oscars.org/oscars/ceremonies/1987|website=Oscars.org|access-date=February 2, 2019}}</ref><ref name="James Ingram - Grammy">{{cite web|title=James Ingram|url=https://www.grammy.com/grammys/artists/james-ingram|website=Grammy.com|access-date=February 2, 2019}}</ref>

In the 1990s, Ingram's highest-profile team-up came again with [[Quincy Jones]], on the song "[[The Secret Garden (Sweet Seduction Suite)|The Secret Garden]]". This song also featured vocals by [[Barry White]], [[El DeBarge]], and [[Al B. Sure!]].<ref name="The Billboard Book of Number One Rhythm & Blues Hits.">{{cite book|last1=White|first1=Adam|last2=Bronson|first2=Fred|title=The Billboard Book of Number One Rhythm & Blues Hits|date=1993|publisher=Billboard Books:Watson-Guptill Publications|location=New York|isbn=9780823082858|page=469}}</ref><ref>{{cite book|last1=Whitburn|first1=Joel|title=Top R&B/Hip-Hop Singles: 1942-2004|date=2004|publisher=Record Search|isbn=978-0898201604}}</ref> Soundtrack songs were popular for Ingram in the 1990s. From the movie ''[[Sarafina! (film)|Sarafina!]]'' came "One More Time", and from ''[[City Slickers]]'' came "Where Did My Heart Go?" In 1991, he and [[Melissa Manchester]] performed the song "The Brightest Star" in the animated Christmas film ''Precious Moments Timmy's Gift''. In 1993, they performed the song again in the film's sequel ''Precious Moments Timmy's Special Delivery''. Ingram's 1994 composition "The Day I Fall in Love", a duet with [[Dolly Parton]], was the theme song for the movie ''[[Beethoven's 2nd (film)|Beethoven's 2nd]]'' and was nominated for an [[Academy Award]] for [[Academy Award for Best Original Song|Best Original Song]].<ref name="1993 Academy Awards® Winners and History">{{cite web|title=1993 Academy Awards® Winners and History|url=https://www.filmsite.org/aa93.html|website=Film Site|access-date=February 2, 2019}}</ref><ref name="Beethoven's 2nd (1993)">{{cite web|title=Beethoven's 2nd (1993)|url=https://catalog.afi.com/Catalog/moviedetails/59461|website=AFI|access-date=February 2, 2019}}</ref> Ingram and Parton performed the song live on the Oscar broadcast. In 1997, he and [[Carnie Wilson]] co-wrote the song "Our Time Has Come" and lent it to the animated film ''[[Cats Don't Dance]]''.<ref name="James Ingram obituary" /><ref name="US R&B singer-songwriter James Ingram dies aged 66" /><ref name="James Ingram, R&B star and Michael Jackson collaborator, dies aged 66" />

During the summer of 2004, Ingram participated in the U.S. television reality show ''[[Celebrity Duets]]'' as a duet partner. Weekly|access-date=February 2, 2019}}</ref> In 2006, Ingram and neo-soul singer [[Angie Stone]] teamed up on "My People".<ref name="My People by Angie Stone">{{cite web|title=My People by Angie Stone|url=https://www.songfacts.com/lyrics/angie-stone/my-people|website=Song Facts|access-date=February 2, 2019}}</ref> In 2011, Ingram joined [[Cliff Richard]]'s list of special guest performers on his ''Soulicious'' Tour performing at various UK venues during November.<ref name="Cliff Richard – review">{{cite web|last1=Michael|first1=Hann|title=Cliff Richard – review|url=https://www.theguardian.com/music/2011/oct/27/cliff-richard-review-soulicious|website=The Guardian|access-date=February 2, 2019}}</ref> He sang two songs from the album with Richard, as well a solo of "Just Once".<ref>{{cite web|title=Cliff Richard – The Soulicious Tour|url=http://www.cliffrichard.org/news/exclusive.htm|website=Cliff Richard Organisation|access-date=June 5, 2012|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120616231532/http://www.cliffrichard.org/news/exclusive.htm|archive-date=June 16, 2012}}</ref> In 2012, Ingram appeared as himself in the ABC television show ''[[Suburgatory]]'', in the episode "The Motherload".<ref name="The Motherload">{{cite web|title=The Motherload|url=https://www.imdb.com/title/tt2246247/|website=[[IMDb]]|access-date=February 2, 2019}}</ref> Also in 2012, he was a guest vocalist at [[Debbie Allen]]'s October 13 live show at the corner of [[Crenshaw, Los Angeles|Crenshaw Blvd. and Martin Luther King Blvd.]] celebrating the arrival of the ''[[Space Shuttle Endeavour]]'', singing [[R. Kelly]]'s "[[I Believe I Can Fly]]".<ref name="Space Shuttle Endeavour Exhibit Opens to the Public at the California Science Center">{{cite web|last1=Battle|first1=Chelsea|title=Space Shuttle Endeavour Exhibit Opens to the Public at the California Science Center|url=https://lasentinel.net/space-shuttle-endeavour-exhibit-opens-to-the-public-at-the-california-science-center.html|website=[[Los Angeles Sentinel]]|access-date=February 2, 2019}}</ref><ref name="James Ingram, R&B Singing Star, Dead At 66">{{cite web|title=James Ingram, R&B Singing Star, Dead At 66|url=http://top40-charts.com/news.php?nid=143605|website=Top-40 Charts|access-date=February 2, 2019}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|last=Rod|first=Pyle|title=Newly Opened Space Shuttle Endeavour Exhibit Thrills California Crowds|url=https://www.space.com/18302-shuttle-endeavour-california-museum-opening.html|website=Space.com|access-date=February 2, 2019}}</ref>

===Death===
Ingram died of brain cancer on January 29, 2019, at the age of 66.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.forbes.com/sites/shanonlee/2019/01/29/rb-legend-james-ingram-dead-at-66-after-battling-brain-cancer/|title=R&B Legend James Ingram Dead At 66 After Battling Brain Cancer|last=Lee|first=Shanon|website=Forbes|language=en|access-date=August 29, 2019}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://metro.co.uk/2019/01/29/michael-jackson-co-writer-james-ingram-dies-aged-66-brain-cancer-battle-8411423/|title=Michael Jackson co-writer James Ingram dies aged 66 after brain cancer|date=January 29, 2019|website=Metro|language=en-US|access-date=August 29, 2019}}</ref>
