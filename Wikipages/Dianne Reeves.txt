'''Dianne Elizabeth Reeves''' (born October 23, 1956) is an American [[jazz]] singer.

== Biography ==
Dianne Reeves was born in [[Detroit, Michigan]], into a musical family. Her father sang, her mother played trumpet, her uncle is bassist [[Charles Burrell (musician)|Charles Burrell]], and her cousin is [[George Duke]]. Her father died when she was two years old, and she was raised in [[Denver, Colorado]], by her mother, Vada Swanson, and maternal family.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.thehistorymakers.org/biography/dianne-reeves#:~:text=Jazz%20singer%20Dianne%20Reeves%20was,family%20members%20in%20Denver%2C%20Colorado.|title=Dianne Reeves {{!}} Biography|website=[[The HistoryMakers]]|date=September 24, 2016|access-date=March 24, 2021}}</ref> 

=== Career ===
In 1971 she started singing and playing piano.<ref name="New Grove">{{cite book| last1=Frederickson| first1=Scott| last2=Kennedy| first2=Gary| editor1-last=Kernfeld |editor1-first=Barry|title=The New Grove Dictionary of Jazz| date=2002| publisher=Grove's Dictionaries Inc.| location=New York| isbn=1-56159-284-6| page=390| volume=3| edition=2nd }}</ref> She was a member of her high school band, and while performing at a convention in Chicago was noticed by trumpeter [[Clark Terry]], who invited her to sing with him. "He had these amazing all-star bands, but I had no idea who they all were! The thing I loved about it was the way they interacted with each other - the kind of intimate exchange that I wasn't part of. For a young singer, it was fertile soil."<ref name="Walters">{{cite web |last1=Walters |first1=John L. |title=Interview: John L Walters meets jazz singer Dianne Reeves |url=https://www.theguardian.com/music/2008/apr/04/jazz.urban |website=The Guardian |access-date=6 September 2019 |date=3 April 2008}}</ref> She studied classical voice at the [[University of Colorado]].<ref name="conversation">{{cite web |title=In Conversation: Dianne Reeves — Rehearsal Magazine |url=http://rehearsalmagazine.com/articles/in-conversation-dianne-reeves |website=Re:hearsal Magazine |access-date=6 September 2019}}</ref>

Reeves moved to [[Los Angeles]], where she sang and recorded with [[Stanley Turrentine]], [[Lenny White]], and [[Billy Childs]].<ref name=HancockInstitute>[https://hancockinstitute.org/meet/dianne-reeves/ "Dianne Reeves"], [[Herbie Hancock Institute of Jazz]].</ref> She recorded with the band [[Caldera (band)|Caldera]],<ref name="Loudon">{{cite web |last1=Loudon |first1=Christopher |title=Dianne Reeves: The JazzTimes Interview |url=https://jazztimes.com/features/interviews/dianne-reeves-the-jazztimes-interview/ |website=JazzTimes |access-date=6 September 2019 |date=9 April 2014}}</ref> then founded the band Night Flight with Billy Childs, with whom she would collaborate again in the 1990s. She moved to New York City and from 1983 to 1986 toured with [[Harry Belafonte]].<ref name="New Grove" />

She signed with [[Blue Note Records|Blue Note]] in 1987 and that year her [[Dianne Reeves (album)|eponymous album]], featuring [[Herbie Hancock]], [[Freddie Hubbard]], and [[Tony Williams (drummer)|Tony Williams]], was nominated for a [[Grammy Award]].<ref name=HancockInstitute /> She went on to win five Grammy Awards.<ref>[http://jazzday.com/artist/reeves-dianne/ "Dianne Reeves"], International Jazz Day, April 30.</ref>

Music critic [[Scott Yanow]] has said of her, "A logical successor to [[Dinah Washington]] and [[Carmen McRae]], Reeves is a superior interpreter of lyrics and a skilled [[scat singer]]."<ref name=AllMusic>{{cite web|author=Scott Yanow |url=http://www.allmusic.com/artist/dianne-reeves-mn0000211570/biography |title=Dianne Reeves |website=AllMusic |date= October 23, 1956 |access-date= February 10, 2014}}</ref> Her sound has been compared to that of [[Patti Austin]], [[Vanessa Rubin]], [[Anita Baker]], and [[Regina Bell]].<ref name=AllMusic />

Reeves performed at the closing ceremony of the [[2002 Winter Olympics]] in [[Salt Lake City]].<ref name=HancockInstitute /> In 2005, she appeared in the film ''[[Good Night, And Good Luck]]'' singing 1950s standards<ref name=AllMusic /> (including "[[How High the Moon]]", "[[I've Got My Eyes on You (1939 song)|I've Got My Eyes on You]]", "[[Too Close for Comfort]]", "[[Straighten Up and Fly Right]]" and "[[One for My Baby]]"). In 2006 the soundtrack won the [[Grammy Award for Best Jazz Vocal Album]].<ref name="Walters" />
