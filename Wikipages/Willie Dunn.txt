'''William Lawrence Dunn''' (August 14, 1941 – August 5, 2013)<ref>{{Cite web |url=https://www.theglobeandmail.com/arts/music/first-nations-troubadour-willie-dunn-sang-truth-to-power/article15038007/ |title=First Nations troubadour Willie Dunn sang truth to power  |date=23 October 2013 |publisher=The Globe and Mail}}</ref> was a [[Canadians|Canadian]] singer-songwriter, film director and politician.<ref>Roy Wright and Andrew McIntosh, [https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/william-lawrence-dunn "Willie Dunn"]. ''[[The Canadian Encyclopedia]]'', February 18, 2008.</ref> Born in [[Montreal]], he was of mixed [[Mi'kmaq people|Mi'kmaq]] and Scottish/Irish background. Dunn often highlighted [[Indigenous peoples of Canada|indigenous]] issues in his work.<ref>Brad Wheeler, [https://www.theglobeandmail.com/arts/music/article-a-new-anthology-celebrates-the-pioneering-indigenous-troubadour/ "A new anthology celebrates the pioneering Indigenous troubadour, filmmaker and activist Willie Dunn"]. ''[[The Globe and Mail]]'', March 24, 2021.</ref>

== Music career ==
Born in [[Montreal]], [[Quebec]], Canada, Dunn was a singer and acoustic guitarist. He released several full-length albums of recorded music including ''Willie Dunn'' (1971), ''The Pacific'' (1980) and ''Metallic'' (1999). ''Metallic'' reprises material from both earlier releases.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.auraltrad.com/willie/ |title=Willie Dunn |publisher=Auraltrad.com |accessdate=2013-08-09}}</ref>  Dunn's most famous song, "I Pity the Country", was a criticism of [[colonialism]] and anti-indigenous racism;<ref>Dave White, [https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/north/airplay-yukon-music-1.5929518 "Trailbreaking Indigenous artist Willie Dunn gets new posthumous release"]. [[CBC North]], February 28, 2021.</ref> he was also known for the song "Son of the Sun", which [[Kashtin]] covered on their second album ''[[Innu (album)|Innu]]''.<ref>Lynn Saxberg, "Kashtin's spirit is infectious". ''[[Ottawa Citizen]]'', September 26, 1991.</ref> In 2004 Dunn released the album ''Son of the Sun'' with sixteen songs (including three live recordings).

His songs "I Pity the Country", "Son of the Sun" and "Peruvian Dream" are featured on the 2014 compilation album ''[[Native North America, Vol. 1]]''.<ref name=exclaim>[http://exclaim.ca/Music/article/light_in_attic_sets_out_to_unearth_forgotten_history_of_first_nations_music_with_native_north_america_compilation "Light in the Attic Unearths the Forgotten History of First Nations Music with 'Native North America' Compilation"]. ''[[Exclaim!]]'', October 8, 2014.</ref>

''Creation Never Sleeps, Creation Never Dies'', a compilation of songs from throughout his career, was released in 2021 on [[Light in the Attic Records]].<ref>Sam Sodomsky, [https://pitchfork.com/reviews/albums/creation-never-sleeps-creation-never-dies-the-willie-dunn-anthology/ "Willie Dunn — Creation Never Sleeps, Creation Never Dies: The Willie Dunn Anthology"]. ''[[Pitchfork Media|Pitchfork]]'', March 22, 2021.</ref>

== Film ==
He wrote a song entitled "[[The Ballad of Crowfoot]]" and directed a ten-minute [[National Film Board of Canada]] (NFB) film of the same name in 1968.<ref>''Montreal Gazette'', October 21, 1990.</ref> Both the song and video are about inhumane and unjust colonial treatment of indigenous Canadians, as well as their taking charge of their destiny and becoming politically active.<ref>''Ottawa Citizen'', 30 July 1992</ref> The first NFB film directed by an indigenous filmmaker,<ref name="fixing">{{cite web|title=Fixing the Gaze: New Indigenous Work at the NFB|url=http://blog.nfb.ca/blog/2017/01/05/fixing-the-gaze-2/|website=NFB/blog|publisher=National Film Board of Canada|accessdate=4 May 2017|date=5 January 2017}}</ref> the film received several awards including a Gold Hugo for best short film at the 1969 [[Chicago International Film Festival]].<ref name="Collections">{{cite web|url=http://www.onf-nfb.gc.ca/eng/collection/film/?id=10462|title=The Ballad of Crowfoot|work=Collections page|publisher=National Film Board of Canada|accessdate=2009-10-02}}</ref><ref name="NFB.ca">{{cite web|url=http://www.nfb.ca/film/ballad_of_crowfoot|title=The Ballad of Willie Dunn|work=Curator's comments by [[Gil Cardinal]]|publisher=National Film Board of Canada|accessdate=2009-10-02}}</ref> His other films include ''The Eagle Project'', ''The Voice of the Land'' and ''Self-Government'',<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.auraltrad.com/willie/info.html|title=Willie Dunn infosite|publisher=Auraltrad.com|accessdate=2013-08-09}}</ref> and his music was used for the films ''[[Incident at Restigouche]]'', about a 1981 police raid on the [[Listuguj Mi'gmaq First Nation]],<ref name="Armitage">{{cite book|last=Armitage|first=Kay|author2=Kass Banning |author3=Brenda Longfellow |author3-link=Brenda Longfellow |author4=Janine Marchessault |author4-link=Janine Marchessault |title=Gendering the Nation: Canadian Women's Cinema|publisher=[[University of Toronto Press]]|date=July 1999|page=83|chapter=The Documentary Practice of Alanis Obomsawin|chapter-url=https://books.google.com/books?id=5_sxWqBMlbQC&q="Incident+at+Restigouche"&pg=PA83|isbn=978-0-8020-7964-0}}</ref> and ''Okanada'', about the 1990 standoff in [[Oka, Quebec]] between police and native protesters.

''The Ballad of Crowfoot'' has sometimes been credited as the first known Canadian [[music video]].<ref name=2020special>[https://www.fyimusicnews.ca/articles/2020/07/22/2020-prism-prize-special-award-winners-announced "2020 Prism Prize Special Award Winners Announced"]. ''FYI Music News'', July 22, 2020.</ref> In 2020 the [[Prism Prize]], Canada's annual award for innovations in music video, introduced a lifetime achievement award named in Dunn's memory, with choreographer and video director [[Laurieann Gibson]] named as the first winner of the award.<ref name=2020special/>

== Politics ==
A longtime member of the [[New Democratic Party (Canada)|New Democratic Party]], Dunn defeated Mohamed Bassuny to win the party's federal nomination for [[Ottawa—Vanier]] in the [[1993 Canadian federal election|1993 federal election]]. He received 3,155 votes (6.50%), finishing fourth against Liberal incumbent [[Jean-Robert Gauthier]].<ref>''Ottawa Citizen'', October 7, 1993.</ref> He participated in the Culturally Diverse First Peoples Arts Showcase tour in 1998,<ref>''Montreal Gazette'', 14 November 1998</ref> and the Nations in a Circle spotlight of 2002.<ref>''Halifax Daily News'', July 25, 2002.</ref> He was inducted into the Aboriginal Walk of Honour in 2005.<ref>''Edmonton Journal'', June 25, 2005.<!-- page?? --></ref> Dunn died in [[Ottawa]] on August 5, 2013, aged 71.<ref name="Dead">{{cite web|author=Doc Rock|url=http://www.thedeadrockstarsclub.com/2013b.html#sthash.F06bms1S.BMMGbcPK.dpbs|title=July to December|publisher=The Dead Rock Stars Club|accessdate=2013-08-09}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.legacy.com/obituaries/ottawacitizen/obituary.aspx?pid=166319038#fbLoggedOut|title=William Dunn obituary|publisher=Legacy.com|date=2013-08-05|accessdate=2013-08-10}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.cbc.ca/news/arts/story/2013/08/09/obit-dunn-willie-singer-filmaker.html|title=Aboriginal singer, activist Willie Dunn dies at 71|publisher=Cbc.ca|accessdate=2013-08-10}}</ref>
