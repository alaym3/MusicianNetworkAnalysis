'''Deniece Williams''' (born '''June Deniece Chandler'''; June 3, 1951)<ref>{{Cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=Fad4hVQ5-FsC&dq=deniece+williams+1951&pg=PA230|title=Hot Hits: Ac Charts 1978-2001|first=Jeffrey Lee|last=Brothers|date=July 7, 2003|publisher=AuthorHouse|isbn=9781410732941|access-date=July 7, 2021|via=Google Books}}</ref><ref>{{Cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=2kkcmS0AzMEC&dq=deniece+williams+1951&pg=PA183|title=Chicago Soul|first=Robert|last=Pruter|date=July 7, 1992|publisher=University of Illinois Press|isbn=9780252062599|access-date=July 7, 2021|via=Google Books}}</ref><ref>{{Cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=eSIhzKnNUf4C&dq=deniece+williams+1951&pg=PA510|title=Dictionary of Pseudonyms: 13,000 Assumed Names and Their Origins, 5th ed.|first=Adrian|last=Room|date=January 10, 2014|publisher=McFarland|isbn=9780786457632|access-date=July 7, 2021|via=Google Books}}</ref> is an American singer, songwriter and record producer. Williams has been described as "one of the great soul voices" by the [[BBC]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.bbc.co.uk/music/reviews/bwzb/|title=Deniece Williams: My Melody|work=BBC}}</ref> She is best known for the songs "[[Free (Deniece Williams song)|Free]]", "[[Silly (song)|Silly]]", "[[It's Gonna Take a Miracle]]" and two ''Billboard'' Hot 100 No.1 singles "[[Let's Hear It for the Boy]]" and "[[Too Much, Too Little, Too Late]]" (with [[Johnny Mathis]]).  Williams has won four [[Grammys]] with twelve nominations altogether.<ref name=Grammy /><ref name=Allmusic>{{cite web|url=https://www.allmusic.com/artist/deniece-williams-mn0000816701/discography|title=Deniece Williams|publisher=AllMusic}}</ref>

== Early  life==
June Deniece Chandler was born and raised in [[Gary, Indiana|Gary]], [[Indiana]], United States.<ref name="LarkinSM">{{cite book|title=[[Encyclopedia of Popular Music|The Guinness Who's Who of Soul Music]]|editor=[[Colin Larkin (writer)|Colin Larkin]]|publisher=[[Guinness Publishing]]|date=1993|edition=First|isbn=0-85112-733-9|pages=298/300}}</ref>

She attended [[Morgan State University]] in [[Baltimore]], [[Maryland]], in the hopes of becoming a registered nurse and an anesthetist, but she dropped out after a year and a half. She recalled, "You have to be a good student to be in college, and I wasn't."<ref name= soulexpress>{{cite web |url= http://www.soulexpress.net/deniecewilliams.htm |title=Deniece Williams Story with Interview | website=Soulexpress.net |access-date=April 30, 2012}}</ref>

==Career==
Williams started performing while a college student, "a part-time job singing at a club, Casino Royal, and I liked it.  It was a lot of fun."{{citation needed|date= January 2021}} During those years, she also worked at a telephone company and as a ward clerk at the [[Mercy Hospital and Medical Center|Chicago Mercy Hospital]].<ref name=soulexpress/>

She recorded for The Toddlin' Tow group of labels as Deniece Chandler. One of those early records, "I'm Walking Away", was released on Toddlin's subsidiary Lock Records in the late 1960s, is a favorite on England's [[Northern Soul]] scene. A brief spell in 1969 with Patti Hamilton's [[the Lovelites]] resulted in her lead on "I'm Not Like the Others", a track that was eventually released in 1999.<ref>[[Herb Kent]], The Kool Gent Presents The Lovelites, Notes to CDretrospective, Love Lite Records, Chicago, Illinois (1999)</ref>

She became a [[backup vocalist]] for [[Stevie Wonder]] as part of "Wonderlove", lending her vocals on his albums ''[[Talking Book]]'', ''[[Fulfillingness' First Finale]]'', and ''[[Songs in the Key of Life]]''. Williams also appeared on [[Syreeta Wright]]'s 1974 album ''[[Stevie Wonder Presents: Syreeta]]'', [[Minnie Riperton]]'s 1974 album ''[[Perfect Angel]]'' and [[Roberta Flack]]'s 1975 album ''[[Feel Like Makin' Love (album)|Feel Like Makin' Love]]''.<ref name= Allmusic />

She left Wonder in 1975 to sign a deal with [[Columbia Records]] and Kalimba Productions. Kalimba was a production company started by [[Maurice White]] and [[Charles Stepney]]. Williams went on to work on her first studio album with both White and Stepney as its producers. Released in 1976 ''[[This Is Niecy]]'' rose to Nos. 3 and 33 on the [[Top R&B/Hip-Hop Albums]] and [[Billboard 200|''Billboard'' 200]] charts.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.45worlds.com/vinyl/album/pc34242|title=Deniece Williams: This Is Niecy|website=45worlds.com}}</ref><ref>{{cite magazine| url=https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/r-b-hip-hop-albums/song/316713|title=Deniece Williams: This is Niecy (Top R&B Hip Hop Albums)|magazine=Billboard}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/billboard-200/song/316713|title=Deniece Williams: This is Niecy (Billboard 200)| website=Billboard.com}}</ref> ''This Is Niecy'' has also been certified Gold in the United States by the [[RIAA]] and silver in the UK by the [[British Phonographic Industry]].<ref>{{cite web| url= https://www.riaa.com/?s=Deniece+Williams|title=Deniece Williams RIAA Certification| website= riaa.com}}</ref><ref>{{cite web| url=https://www.bpi.co.uk/award/7556-2591-2|title=BPI Certification for Deniece Williams: This Is Niecy| website= BPI.co.uk}}</ref> An album cut, "[[Free (Deniece Williams song)|Free]]", got to nos. 1, 2 & 25 on the [[UK Singles Chart|UK Singles]], ''[[Billboard (magazine)|Billboard]]'' Hot R&B Songs and Hot 100charts respectively. Another single being "That's What Friends Are For" rose to No. 8 on the UK Singles chart.<ref>{{cite web| url= https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/r-b-hip-hop-songs/song/337479|title=Deniece Williams: Free (Hot R&B/Hip-Hop Songs)| website=Billboard.com}}</ref><ref>{{cite magazine|url=https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/hot-100/song/337479|title=Deniece Williams: Free (Hot 100)|magazine=Billboard}}</ref><ref name=OC>{{cite web| url=http://www.officialcharts.com/artist/16142/deniece-williams/|title=Deniece Williams (Singles) |website= OfficialCharts.com}}</ref>

A few months before the release of ''This Is Niecy'', Charles Stepney died. White went on to solely produce Williams' second album, ''[[Song Bird (Deniece Williams album)|Song Bird]]'', released in 1977.<ref>{{cite web| url=http://www.45worlds.com/vinyl/album/jc34911|title=Deniece Williams: Songbird| website=45worlds.com}}</ref> The album rose to No. 23 on the ''Billboard'' [[Top R&B/Hip-Hop Albums|Top Soul Albums]] chart.<ref name=BBD>{{cite magazine| url=https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/BLP/song/315900|title=Deniece Williams: Songbird (Top R&B Albums) |magazine=Billboard}}</ref> ''[[The Daily Express]]'' noted that Songbird is "filled with gorgeous songs that showcase her four-octave range to full effect".<ref>{{cite web| url= https://www.express.co.uk/entertainment/music/191598/Review-Deniece-Williams-Song-Bird-BBR/amp| title= Review: Deniece Williams - Song Bird (BBR)| author=Gage, Simon|date=August 6, 2010| website=[[Daily Express]]| via= express.co.uk}}</ref> John Rockwell of ''[[The New York Times]]'' also called the album "most appealing" and "refreshingly varied".<ref>{{cite web| url= https://www.nytimes.com/1977/12/30/archives/the-pop-life-john-rockwell.html| title=The Pop Life| date=December 30, 1977| author=Rockwell, John|author-link=John Rockwell|website=[[The New York Times]]| via=nytimes.com}}</ref>
A song from the album called "[[Baby, Baby My Love's All for You]]", reached No. 13 on the ''Billboard'' Hot Soul Songs chart and No. 32 UK Singles chart.<ref name="OC" /><ref>{{cite magazine| url= https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/BSI/song/373983 |title=Deniece Williams: Baby, Baby My Love's All For You (Hot R&B/Hip-Hop Songs)|magazine=Billboard}}</ref>

Williams guested on Roberta Flack's 1977 album, ''[[Blue Lights in the Basement]]'',<ref name= Allmusic /> and sang along with Maurice White on [[Weather Report]]'s 1978 album, ''[[Mr. Gone (album)|Mr. Gone]]''.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.jazzwisemagazine.com/artists/14523-weather-report-the-group-on-record|title=Weather Report: the life and times of the group on record| website=JazzWiseMagazine.com}}</ref><ref>{{cite web| url=http://www.45worlds.com/vinyl/album/jc35358|title=Weather Report: Mr. Gone |website=45worlds.com}}</ref>

Kalimba Productions.<ref>{{cite work |title=Johnny Mathis and Deniece Williams: That's What Friends Are For|date=1978|publisher=[[Columbia Records]]}}</ref> The LP rose to No. 14 on the ''Billboard'' William's fourth studio recording, ''[[When Love Comes Calling (Deniece Williams album)|When Love Comes Calling]]'' was issued in 1979 by [[ARC Records]] (ARC), Maurice White's [[vanity label]] on Columbia.<ref>{{cite work|title=Deniece Williams: When Love Comes Calling|date=1979|publisher=[[ARC Records|ARC]]/[[Columbia Records]]}}</ref> The album rose to No. 27 on the ''Billboard'' Top Soul Albums chart.<ref>{{cite magazine|url=https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/BLP/song/314437|title=Deniece Williams: When Love Comes Calling (Top Soul Albums)|magazine=Billboard}}</ref> A single from the album, "[[I've Got the Next Dance]]", rose to No. 1 on the ''Billboard'' Dance/Club Play chart.<ref>{{cite magazine|url=https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/DSI/song/373184|title=Deniece Williams: I've Got The Next Dance|magazine=Billboard}}</ref>

Williams issued her follow-up album, ''[[My Melody (Deniece Williams album)|My Melody]]'', in 1981 on Columbia/ARC.<ref>{{cite work|title=Deniece Williams: My Melody|date=1981|publisher=[[ARC Records|ARC]]/[[Columbia Records]]}}</ref> The album rose to No. 13 on the ''[[Billboard (magazine)|Billboard]]'' [[Top R&B/Hip-Hop Albums|Top Soul Albums]] chart.<ref>{{cite magazine|url=https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/BLP/song/377025|title=Deniece Williams: My Melody (Top Soul Albums)|magazine=Billboard}}</ref> My Melody has been certified [[Music recording certification|Gold]] in the US by the [[RIAA]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.riaa.com/gold-platinum/?tab_active=default-award&ar=Deniece+Williams&ti=My+melody&lab=&genre=&format=&date_option=release&from=&to=&award=&type=&category=&adv=SEARCH#search_section|title=Deniece Williams: My Melody|publisher=[[RIAA]]|website=riaa.com}}</ref>
The single, "[[Silly (song)|Silly]]", reached No. 11 on the ''Billboard'' Hot Soul Songs chart.<ref>{{cite magazine|url=https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/BSI/song/335060|title=Deniece Williams: Silly (Hot Soul Songs)|magazine=Billboard}}</ref>

Williams issued her follow-up album, ''[[Niecy]]'', in 1982 on Columbia/ARC.<ref>{{cite work|title=Deniece Williams: Niecy|date=1982|publisher=[[ARC Records|ARC]]/[[Columbia Records]]}}</ref> ''Niecy'' rose to No. 20 on the ''Billboard'' 200 and No. 5 on the ''Billboard'' Top Soul Albums charts.<ref>{{cite magazine|url=https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/BLP/song/312865|title=Deniece Williams: Niecy (Top Soul Albums)|magazine=Billboard}}</ref><ref>{{cite magazine| url=https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/TLP/song/312865|title=Deniece Williams: Niecy (Billboard 200)|magazine= Billboard |via=billboard.com}}</ref>

''[[People (magazine)|People]]'' described the album as "upbeat, soulful and polished".<ref>{{cite web|url=https://people.com/archive/picks-and-pans-review-niecy-vol-17-no-20/|title=Picks and Pans Review: Niecy| date=May 24, 1982|website=[[People (magazine)|People]]| via= people.com}}</ref>

A cover of [[the Royalettes]]' "[[It's Gonna Take a Miracle]]" reached No. 1 on the ''Billboard'' Hot Soul Songs chart and No. 10 on the ''Billboard'' Hot 100 chart.<ref>{{cite magazine| url= https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/BSI/song/334579| title=Deniece Williams: It's Gonna Take a Miracle (Hot Soul Songs)|magazine=[[Billboard (magazine)|Billboard]]| via=billboard.com}}</ref><ref>{{cite web| url=https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/HSI/song/334579|title=Deniece Williams: It's Gonna Take a Miracle (Hot 100)| work= Billboard| via=billboard.com}}</ref> Williams and Mathis collaborated once again to record "Without Us" the theme song of the sitcom ''[[Family Ties]]'' which debuted in September 1982.<ref>{{cite web| url=https://m.imdb.com/title/tt0083413/|title=Family Ties|publisher=IMDb}}</ref>

In the Netherlands the album track "It's Your Conscience" had been released as a single. It climbed to No. 15 on the [[Dutch Top 40]] charts.<ref>{{cite web |url= https://www.top40.nl/deniece-williams/deniece-williams-it-s-your-conscience-8892|title=Nederlandse Top 40|publisher=Stichting Nederlandse Top 40}}</ref>

During 1983 she released her seventh studio album, ''[[I'm So Proud]]'', which got to No. 10 on the Billboard Top R&B Albums chart. I'm So Proud was also nominated for a Grammy in the [[Best R&B Vocal Performance, Female]] category.<ref name= Allmusic /><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/BLP/song/312592| title=Deniece Williams: I'm So Proud (Top R&B Albums)| work=[[Billboard (magazine)|Billboard]]| via=billboard.com}}</ref><ref name= Grammy /> During 1984 Williams released her follow up album ''[[Let's Hear It for the Boy (album)|Let's Hear It for the Boy]]''. The title track reached No. 1 on the ''Billboard'' Hot 100 and was featured on the soundtrack to ''[[Footloose (1984 film)|Footloose]]''. She later guested on [[Johnny Mathis]]' 1984 album [[A Special Part of Me]], Stevie Wonder's 1985 LP, ''[[In Square Circle]]'' and [[James Taylor]]'s 1985 album ''[[That's Why I'm Here]]''.<ref name= Allmusic />

Although Williams had recorded one inspirational song on almost each of her mainstream albums, it was in 1980 that her musical career path began to change favoring Gospel music. Williams joined with friends [[Philip Bailey]] of [[Earth, Wind & Fire]] fame, [[Billy Davis (songwriter)|Billy Davis]] and [[Marilyn McCoo]] to present a gospel show at [[Roxy Theatre (West Hollywood)|The Roxy]], a popular Los Angeles club: "Jesus at the Roxy". Williams later reported that "God did something miraculous. Over three hundred people were saved."<ref>As reported in an interview Williams gave to ''Gospel Today'' magazine.</ref>  In 1985, at the [[27th Annual Grammy Awards]], Williams sang an [[a cappella]] version of her 1977 composition "God Is Amazing", a Gospel song, rather than her No. 1 song "Let's Hear It for the Boy", much to her record company's disdain.<ref name=Show />

During 1986, her first gospel studio album, ''So Glad I Know'', was released on [[Sparrow Records]],<ref name="LarkinSM"/> and got to No. 8 on the ''Billboard'' Top Christian Albums chart and was nominated for [[Grammy Award for Best Gospel Vocal Performance, Female|Best Gospel Performance, Female Grammy]]. "They Say", a duet with [[Sandi Patti]], Williams won the [[Grammy Award for Best Gospel Vocal Performance by a Duo or Group, Choir or Chorus]] and for "I Surrender All", she won the [[Grammy Award for Best Soul Gospel Performance, Female]].<ref name= Allmusic /><ref>{{cite magazine| url= https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/ILL/song/327313| title=Deniece Williams: So Glad I Know (Top Christian Albums)|magazine=[[Billboard (magazine)|Billboard]]}}</ref><ref name=Show>{{cite web|url=http://www.sacculturalhub.com/item/10579-exclusive-deniece-williams-preps-for-oakland-shows-this-weekend-remembers-collaborations-with-mathis-natalie-cole-and-sandi-patty| title= EXCLUSIVE: Deniece Williams Preps For Oakland Shows This Weekend, Remembers Collaborations With Mathis, Natalie Cole, and Sandi Patty |website= Saccculturalhub.com}}</ref><ref name=Grammy />

During 1987 she released her tenth studio album, ''Water Under the Bridge'', which rose to No. 39 on the Billboard Top R&B Albums chart. "Never Say Never" reached No. 6 on the ''Billboard'' Hot R&B Songs chart and No. 23 on the Billboard [[Dance Club Songs]] chart.<ref name= Allmusic /><ref>{{cite magazine| url= https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/BLP/song/376542| title=Deniece Williams: Water Under The Bridge (Top R&B Albums)| magazine= Billboard}}</ref><ref>{{cite magazine| url= https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/DSI/song/351330|title=Deniece Williams: Never Say Never (Dance Club Songs)|magazine=Billboard}}</ref><ref>{{cite magazine|url=https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/BSI/song/369195|title=Deniece Williams: Never Say Never (Hot R&B Songs)|magazine=Billboard}}</ref>

A year later she released her follow-up studio album, ''As Good As It Gets'', which rose to No. 48 on the Billboard Top R&B Albums chart. "I Can't Wait" charted at No. 8 on the Billboard Hot R&B Songs chart. Another single, "This Is As Good As It Gets", rose to No. 29 on the ''Billboard'' Hot R&B Songs chart.<ref name= Allmusic /><ref>{{cite magazine| url= https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/BLP/song/376053| title=Deniece Williams: As Good As It Gets (Top R&B Albums)|magazine=Billboard}}</ref><ref>{{cite magazine| url= https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/BSI/song/368391|title=Deniece Williams: I Can't Wait (Hot R&B Songs)|magazine=Billboard}}</ref><ref>{{cite magazine|url=https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/BSI/song/368540|title=Deniece Williams: This Is As Good As It Gets (Hot R&B Songs)|magazine=Billboard}}</ref>

During 1989, she issued her second Gospel album ''Special Love''.<ref>{{cite work|title=Deniece Williams: Special Love|date=1989|publisher=[[Sparrow Records]]}}</ref>
That album reached No. 11 on the ''Billboard'' Top Christian Albums chart.<ref>{{cite magazine| url= https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/ILL/song/327502|title=Deniece Williams: Special Love (Top Christian Albums)|magazine=Billboard}}</ref>

Williams went on to appear on [[Nancy Wilson (jazz singer)|Nancy Wilson]]'s 1990 LP [[A Lady with a Song]], [[George Duke]]'s 1992 album Snapshot, [[Stevie Wonder]]'s 1995 LP [[Conversation Peace]] and [[Spyro Gyra]]'s 1995 album [[Love & Other Obsessions]]. During 1999 she released another Gospel album, ''[[This Is My Song (Deniece Williams album)|This Is My Song]]'', on [[Harmony Records]]. The album rose to No. 14 on the ''Billboard'' [[Top Gospel Albums]] chart. As well This Is My Song won a [[Grammy Award for Best Pop/Contemporary Gospel Album]].<ref name= Allmusic /><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/gospel-albums/song/181289|title=Deniece Williams: This Is My Song (Top Gospel Albums)|work=Billboard}}</ref><ref name=Grammy />

She later released another studio album entitled ''Love, Niecy Style'', produced by Philly Soul veteran [[Bobby Eli]], in April 2007 on Shanachie Records.<ref name= Allmusic /> Love, Niecy Style rose to No. 41 on the ''Billboard''  R&B/Hip Hop Albums chart.<ref>{{cite magazine |url= https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/r-b-hip-hop-albums/song/527344 |title= Deniece Williams: Love, Niecy Style (Top R&B/Hip-Hop Albums) |magazine= Billboard}}</ref> On October 13, 2007, Williams performed in her hometown of Gary, Indiana at the re-opening of the historic Glen Theater.<ref>{{cite web |url= https://www.nwitimes.com/news/local/curtain-rises-on-new-glen-theater/article_200b86fc-c03d-51a4-b3ff-1b915d37f577.html|title=Curtain rises on new Glen Theater |website= nwitimes.com}}</ref> Williams was thereafter recognized by Indiana State Representative [[Vernon G. Smith]] as an Outstanding Hoosier.<ref name=IMDb>{{cite web |url= https://m.imdb.com/name/nm0930415/?ref_=m_nmbio_bio_nm |title=Deniece Williams |publisher= IMDb}}</ref>

During October 2007 Williams went on to issue a single called, "Grateful: The Rededication", with Wanda Vaughn of [[The Emotions]] and Sherree Brown. The single got to No. 40 on the ''Billboard'' [[Adult R&B Songs]] chart.<ref>{{cite magazine|url=https://www.billboard.com/music/deniece-williams/chart-history/hot-adult-r-and-b-airplay/song/534911|title=Wanda Vaughn, Deniece Williams & Sherree Brown: Grateful (Adult R&B Songs)|magazine=Billboard}}</ref> On April 29, 2008, Williams announced that she was preparing a proposal to establish a program called KOPâ€”Kids of Promiseâ€”in her hometown of [[Gary, Indiana]]. == Filmography ==
Williams went on to appear in the 2003 holiday movie ''[[Christmas Child]]''. During December 2005 she appeared on the reality-dating show ''[[Elimidate]]'' as part of their "Celebrity Week".<ref name=IMDb />

== Vocal profile ==
Deniece Williams has a four-octave range and distinctive soprano voice.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.soulexpress.net/deniecewilliams.htm|title=Deniece Williams Story with Interview|work=soulexpress.net}}</ref> Her vocal range was also pointed out by ''[[The New York Times]]'', "Miss Williams mounted a spectacular vocal display in which her penetrating, feline [[soprano]] soared effortlessly to E flat above high C, and she worked various vowel sounds into prolonged feats of vocal gymnastics."<ref>{{cite news|last=Holden |first=Stephen |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1982/10/11/arts/pop-soul-deniece-williams-in-concert.html |title=Pop-Soul â€“ Deniece Williams in Concert |newspaper=[[The New York Times]] |date=October 11, 1982 |access-date=April 2, 2017}}</ref> In pointing to Williams's similar vocal ability as her former musical icon and colleague ([[Minnie Riperton]]), Mark Anthony Neal, in referencing [[Jill Scott]]'s agility in displaying vocal acrobatics, states, "Scott draws on her upper register recalling the artistry of the late Minnie Riperton and "songbird" Deniece Williams."<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.popmatters.com/scottjill-experience-2496067960.html|title=Jill Scott: Experience: Jill Scott 826+|website=PopMatters}}</ref> According to Monica Haynes of Post-Gazette.com, Williams "has the kind of range that would make [[Mariah Carey]] quiver".<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.post-gazette.com/pg/07099/776305-388.stm|title=Music Review: Time just can't stop energized O'Jays train|work=Pittsburgh Post-Gazette}}</ref>

==Personal life==
Williams has been married three times and has four sons. Williams was married to her middle school sweetheart Kendrick Williams from 1971 until 1975, before she relocated to California in the early 1970s. Together they had two sons, Kendrick Jr. (b. 1972) and Kevin (b. 1973). In May 1981, Williams married actor and minister Christipher Joy. Williams and Joy separated in August 1982,<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=9rcDAAAAMBAJ&dq=deniece+williams+husband&pg=PA59|title=Jet|date=August 30, 1982|publisher=Johnson Publishing Company|access-date=July 7, 2021|via=Google Books}}</ref> divorcing later that year.<ref>{{Cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=tgs8AQAAIAAJ&q=deniece+williams+husband+brad+westering|title=Contemporary Musicians|date=July 7, 1989|publisher=Gale Research, Incorporated|isbn=9780810322110|access-date=July 7, 2021|via=Google Books}}</ref> From 1986 until 1993, Williams was married to Brad Westering,<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=3NQDAAAAMBAJ&dq=deniece+williams+husband&pg=PA156|title=Ebony|date=March 7, 1989|publisher=Johnson Publishing Company|access-date=July 7, 2021|via=Google Books}}</ref> with whom she had two sons: Forrest (b. 1988)<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=5q4DAAAAMBAJ&dq=deniece+williams+kids&pg=PA36|title=Jet|date=October 17, 1988|publisher=Johnson Publishing Company|access-date=July 7, 2021|via=Google Books}}</ref> and Logan.
