'''Dua Saleh''' ({{IPA-ar|duˈʕæːʔ|IPA}} {{needs IPA}}<ref>{{cite AV media |url=https://www.facebook.com/doitlikedua/posts/2843427569209503|publisher=Dua Saleh|title=Spotify Presents: Pollen: Dua Saleh|via=Facebook|date=September 8, 2020|accessdate=September 16, 2020}}</ref><ref>{{cite AV media |url=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=17bNjmajHfk|publisher=Button Poetry|title=White People Love Fractions|via=Youtube|date=June 4, 2020|accessdate=September 16, 2020}}</ref>) is a Sudanese-American singer based in [[Minneapolis]], [[Minnesota]]. Their debut [[extended play|EP]] ''Nūr'' was released in January 2019 by the Against Giants record label to critical acclaim, with their second, ''Rosetta'', released in June the following year.

Saleh, who came to the United States from [[Sudan]] as a child, wrote poetry from an early age. While attending [[Augsburg University]], they began to experiment with music, eventually releasing a demo in 2017. Shortly after, they began to work with local producer [[Psymun]] and began performing their music live, culminating in their 2019 EP release. Their music has been characterized as rap, pop, and R&B, although they have also been described as defying genres. In addition to writing and recording music, Saleh has also acted in [[List of theaters in Minnesota|theater in Minneapolis]] and is known for their recurring role as Cal Bowman in the [[Netflix]] series ''[[Sex Education (TV series)|Sex Education]]''.

==Career==
Saleh began writing poetry when they were four years old and performed at their first [[open mic]] in their senior year of high school.<ref name="mndaily">{{cite news |last1=Gorinshteyn |first1=Ksenia |title=Finding identity, Dua Saleh morphs poetry into lyricism |url=https://www.mndaily.com/article/2020/02/ae-finding-identity-dua-saleh-morphs-poetry-into-lyricism |accessdate=February 9, 2020 |work=[[Minnesota Daily]] |publisher=[[University of Minnesota]] |date=February 2, 2020 |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20200209233318/https://www.mndaily.com/article/2020/02/ae-finding-identity-dua-saleh-morphs-poetry-into-lyricism |archivedate=February 9, 2020}}</ref> They used poetry to process experiences in their life and continued performing around the [[Minneapolis–Saint Paul]] area while in school at Augsburg.<ref name="hamline oracle" /> Their poem "Pins and Needles" was recorded and shared on [[Button Poetry]]'s [[YouTube]] channel where it garnered tens of thousands of views by 2017 and over one hundred thousand by 2018.<ref name="p2c" /><ref name="first take">{{cite news |last1=Johnson |first1=Cecilia |title=Song premiere: Dua nails their 'First Take' |url=https://blog.thecurrent.org/2017/05/song-premiere-dua-nails-their-first-take/ |accessdate=February 8, 2020 |work=[[89.3 The Current]] |date=May 10, 2017 |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20191115064239/https://blog.thecurrent.org/2017/05/song-premiere-dua-nails-their-first-take/ |archivedate=November 15, 2019}}</ref> They have also performed as part of the Queer Voices series at [[Intermedia Arts]].<ref name="queer voices">{{cite news |last1=Eler |first1=Alicia |title=What would be lost if Intermedia closes? Twin Cities artists reflect |url=http://www.startribune.com/what-would-be-lost-if-intermedia-closes-twin-cities-artists-reflect/451893793/ |accessdate=February 8, 2020 |work=[[Star Tribune]] |date=October 21, 2017 |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20180821221932/http://www.startribune.com/what-would-be-lost-if-intermedia-closes-twin-cities-artists-reflect/451893793/ |archivedate=August 21, 2018}}</ref>

While studying at Augsburg and working full-time, Saleh began to find that poetry was not giving them "enough nurturing sustenance".<ref name="mndaily" /> They said of this time that "I was trying to do way too much, and my body was just like, 'No, you need to release something.' The melodies started coming in, and I think that was my body reacting and wanting to heal me, in some ways."<ref name="mndaily" /> Saleh did not consider themself to be a proficient singer but connected via a mutual friend to producer Mike Frey to experiment with recording.<ref name="first take" /> In Frey's home studio, he played a four-[[Bar (music)|bar]] beat that Saleh mumbled over until Saleh announced they were ready to record; Frey expected four or eight bars from Saleh but Saleh ended up performing an entire track in a single [[take]], with Frey manually extending the beat to keep up as Saleh sang.<ref name="first take" /> According to Frey, the resulting single take was "such a good performance that we decided this is what we're going to run with"; it was released on May 10, 2017, as "First Take", Saleh's first [[Single (music)|single]], under the stage name Dua.<ref name="first take" /><ref>{{cite news |last1=Jagota |first1=Vrinda |title=5 Muslim Artists to Follow for Muslim Women's Day |url=https://www.papermag.com/5-muslim-artists-to-follow-2553725083.html |accessdate=February 14, 2020 |work=[[Paper (magazine)|Paper]] |date=March 27, 2018 |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20190708113903/https://www.papermag.com/5-muslim-artists-to-follow-2553725083.html |archivedate=July 8, 2019}}</ref>

Following the single's release, Minneapolis producer [[Psymun]] reached out to Saleh about working together; this surprised Saleh who was familiar with Psymun's work but thought he was based in the United Kingdom.<ref name="yn2k" /><ref name="nur current interview">{{cite news |last1=Johnson |first1=Cecilia |title=Interview: Dua Saleh talks 'Nūr,' Psymun, and 2018 takeaways |url=https://blog.thecurrent.org/2019/01/interview-dua-saleh-talks-nur-psymun-and-2018-takeaways/ |accessdate=February 9, 2020 |work=[[89.3 The Current]] |date=January 17, 2019 |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20190819022607/https://blog.thecurrent.org/2019/01/interview-dua-saleh-talks-nur-psymun-and-2018-takeaways/ |archivedate=August 19, 2019}}</ref> "First Take" was well-received online and was followed by the release of Saleh's first Psymun-produced single, a performance at the [[Eaux Claires]] festival, and a collaboration with Florida rapper Chester Watson ("Void Interlude").<ref name="p2c" />

Saleh lived what they described as a "super-sheltered" childhood and grew up listening mostly to older [[Sudanese music]] and [[Nancy Ajram]].<ref name="just getting citypages" /> In high school, when they got a laptop, they listened to a great deal of [[1940s in jazz|jazz from the 1940s]] and later turned their attention to [[Hip hop music#1990s|1990s hip hop]].<ref name="just getting citypages" /> Subsequently, Saleh "went through a year or two of finding as many queer artists as [they] could", citing [[Mykki Blanco]], [[Le1f]], and [[Kilo Kish]] among the artists during this period who "saved [their] life".<ref name="just getting citypages" /> They have also cited the music and lives of [[Sister Rosetta Tharpe]] and [[Marie Knight]] as influences, including naming their second EP after Tharpe.<ref name="i-D" />

Saleh said that among other reasons for making music, {{nowrap|"[i]n}} Sudan, there's a lot of queer, trans and non-binary people who are closeted, so I try to put out as much content that's like, the gay and trans agenda, as possible!"<ref name="i-D" /> On ''Rosetta'', Saleh released the track "Smut", sung partially in Arabic, hoping "to break into the Sudani market" and connect with Sudanese listeners.<ref name="them" />

Saleh's music has been categorized as rap,<ref name="just getting citypages" /><ref name="rc nur" /> pop,<ref name="pitchfork nur" /> and [[Rhythm and blues|R&B]].<ref name="pitchfork nur" /> Christgau described Saleh's performance style on ''Nūr'' as rap-singing while Michelle Kim characterized their vocal performance style as alternating "between deliciously tactile rapping and spellbinding singing".<ref name="them" /><ref name="rc nur" /> Saleh also been noted for defying genre in their music.<ref name="them" /><ref name="nur current interview" /><ref name="rc nur" />

==Personal life==
Saleh's first language was Arabic, which they said in 2017 was a "struggle to maintain" because of their immersion in English.<ref name="yn2k" /> Saleh is Muslim, but they do not consider themself "religious in the institutional sense".<ref name="them" /> They have discussed their relationship with traditions of [[Islamic clothing|Islamic attire]], stating that traditional "attire isn't reflective of [their] personal relationship with the [[Din (Arabic)|deen]], but reflective of the image of a Muslim that [they've] been indoctrinated to believe is socially accepted".<ref>{{cite news |last1=Taylor |first1=Vanessa |title=5 Black Muslim Women On Why #BlackOutEid Is Important |url=https://www.thefader.com/2017/06/21/blackout-eid-black-muslims-reflections |accessdate=February 8, 2020 |work=[[The Fader]] |date=June 21, 2017 |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20190920111700/https://www.thefader.com/2017/06/21/blackout-eid-black-muslims-reflections |archivedate=September 20, 2019}}</ref> In high school, they were elected vice president of Central's [[gay–straight alliance]]; when their mother discovered pamphlets for the organization in their backpack, she sent Saleh to Dugsi Academy, an Islamic charter school in Saint Paul.<ref name="them" />

In addition to writing and performing music and poetry, Saleh has also acted, including in 20% Theatre Company's November 2018 production of ''Waafrika 123'' in Minneapolis.<ref name="p2c" />

Saleh is [[nonbinary|non-binary]] and uses [[Singular they|they/them]], [[He (pronoun)|he/him]], and [[Xe (pronoun)|xe/xyr]] pronouns.<ref name="accidental strib" /><ref>{{cite tweet|last=Saleh|first=Dua|user=doitlikedua|number=1315366612598480897|date=October 11, 2020|title=hey I know I’m already out and I love the pronouns that I use they/them/theirs & he/him/his but I’ve also been referring to myself with the neopronouns xe/xyr/xim #NationalComingOutDay|accessdate=October 11, 2020|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20201012002319/https://twitter.com/doitlikedua/status/1315366612598480897|archivedate=October 12, 2020|url-status=live}}</ref> They [[coming out|came out]] while in their second year at Augsburg but have said they "always been on gay shit".<ref name="them" /> They live in Minneapolis.<ref name="nur current interview" />
